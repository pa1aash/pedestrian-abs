\title{When Steering Paradigms Fail: A Diagnostic Ablation of Force-Based, Anticipatory, and Velocity-Obstacle Pedestrian Models with Density-Adaptive Mitigation}

\author{\authorname{Anonymous Authors}
\affiliation{}
\email{}
}

\keywords{Crowd Simulation, Steering Ablation, Social Force Model, Collision Avoidance, Crowd Safety, Empirical Calibration.}

\abstract{No single pedestrian steering paradigm performs well across all crowd scenarios. We present a diagnostic ablation of Social Force (SFM), time-to-collision (TTC), and Optimal Reciprocal Collision Avoidance (ORCA) steering across bottleneck, bidirectional, and crossing geometries ($n=25$ per cell) and identify two paradigm-specific failure modes. At a 0.8\,m bottleneck, neither plain SFM nor SFM+TTC evacuates any of 25 trials, while ORCA-enabled configurations evacuate 52--56\%; a symmetry-breaking control locates the noise threshold for deadlock resolution at $\sigma_{50}=0.049$\,m/s (95\% CI $[0.044, 0.053]$), and within $n=25$ passive Gaussian noise is indistinguishable from ORCA's LP-based velocity selection. ORCA yielding produces gridlock in counterflow. A density-modulated additive blend in which only the ORCA contribution is density-weighted serves as a mitigation probe for each failure mode. Zone-decomposition shows TTC reduces upstream queue-formation contact-overlaps by 30\% (NB GLM IRR $=0.695$, $p_{\mathrm{HS}}<0.001$), concentrated upstream rather than at the throat. At 90\textdegree{} crossings the blend triples throughput over plain SFM (17.1 of 200 vs.\ 5.9 agents). Held-out bottleneck flow rates are underestimated by 26--43\%. Code and data are released.}

\section{\uppercase{Introduction}}
\label{sec:introduction}

Crowd disasters remain a persistent threat to public safety. The 2022 Itaewon crush killed 159 people in a narrow alley~\cite{Lee24}; the 2010 Love Parade disaster killed 21 at a tunnel bottleneck~\cite{Helbing12}. These tragedies share a pattern: localised density exceeds survivable thresholds before organisers detect the hazard. Predictive tools that anticipate dangerous density buildups and evaluate mitigations \emph{before} an event are therefore critical.

This paper is a \textbf{diagnostic ablation study}, not a new-method paper. We do not propose a production steering model. We ask where and why each of the three dominant pedestrian steering paradigms --- Social Force (SFM)~\cite{Helbing95,Helbing00}, time-to-collision anticipation (TTC)~\cite{Karamouzas14}, and velocity-obstacle / reciprocal avoidance (ORCA)~\cite{Fiorini98,VandenBerg11} --- fails when isolated, and use a density-modulated additive blend (in which only the ORCA term is density-weighted) as a \emph{mitigation probe} to test whether each failure mode can be attenuated without runtime scenario classification.

The diagnostic framing is motivated by a gap in the ablation literature: single-paradigm comparisons are common, but zone-decomposed attribution of where each paradigm's failure is localised (upstream queue, throat, or downstream) has not, to our knowledge, been reported for the SFM/TTC/ORCA triad on a common set of geometries.

Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{zone-decomposed ablation} of SFM, TTC, and ORCA across bottleneck, bidirectional, and crossing geometries that identifies two paradigm-specific failure modes observed in our experiments: TTC anticipation does not break symmetric arches at narrow exits (0/25 evacuations for both C1 and C2 at $w=0.8$\,m), and ORCA-style reciprocal yielding depresses throughput in counterflow.
    \item A \textbf{symmetry-breaking control experiment} ($C_1+\varepsilon$) that characterises the noise threshold for deadlock resolution ($\sigma_{50}\approx 0.049$\,m/s, 95\% CI $[0.044, 0.053]$) and establishes that within $n=25$ passive Gaussian velocity noise is indistinguishable from ORCA's LP-based velocity selection as a deadlock-breaking mechanism; larger-$n$ separation is future work.
    \item Evaluation of a \textbf{density-modulated additive blend} of the three paradigms (only the ORCA contribution is density-weighted) as a mitigation probe, calibrated phenomenologically against 4776 FZJ unidirectional corridor points and benchmarked against JuPedSim as a qualitative sanity check. We acknowledge a systematic $-26\%$ to $-43\%$ out-of-distribution underestimation on held-out FZJ bottleneck flow rates and do not claim the blend generalises quantitatively across geometries.
\end{enumerate}

Section~\ref{sec:related} reviews related work. Section~\ref{sec:framework} presents the methodology. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} describes the experiments: FD calibration, OOD validation, density-adaptive gate, zone-decomposed ablation, deadlock with $C_1+\varepsilon$ control, external-simulator comparison, and computational performance. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses findings and limitations. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes.

%% SECTION 2: RELATED WORK

\section{\uppercase{Related Work}}
\label{sec:related}

\textbf{Force-based and velocity-based models.}
The Social Force Model (SFM) of Helbing and Moln\'{a}r~\cite{Helbing95} represents pedestrians as particles subject to desired, social-repulsive, and contact forces; the extended model~\cite{Helbing00} adds body compression and sliding friction for dense-crowd simulation. Reynolds~\cite{Reynolds99} proposed steering behaviours for autonomous agents, and Fiorini and Shiller~\cite{Fiorini98} formalised velocity obstacles. Van den Berg et al.~\cite{VandenBerg11} introduced Optimal Reciprocal Collision Avoidance (ORCA), which solves a 2D linear program over half-plane velocity constraints. Karamouzas et al.~\cite{Karamouzas14} showed pedestrians respond to anticipated time-to-collision with an inverse power-law force. Guy et al.~\cite{Guy10} proposed PLEdestrians, optimising agent parameters to match real trajectories.

These models are typically deployed individually. Force-based approaches handle contact-rich scenarios well but produce oscillatory paths at low density; velocity-based methods yield smooth trajectories but cannot represent physical compression.

\textbf{Hybrid and multi-regime models.}
Van Toll et al.~\cite{VanToll21} combine agents with smoothed particle hydrodynamics for extreme-density crowds. Moussa\"{\i}d et al.~\cite{Moussaid11} develop heuristic models calibrated to bottleneck data, though single-paradigm. Cristiani et al.~\cite{Cristiani23} present a multi-scale simulator handling all density regimes via model switching. Gaudou et al.~\cite{Gaudou24} survey agent-based crowd simulation. Existing hybrids typically combine at most two paradigms and use discrete switching. We do not find prior work that (i) blends SFM contact, TTC anticipation, and ORCA LP velocities within a single per-agent step with a continuous density weight, and (ii) decomposes failure modes by spatial zone.

\textbf{Safety metrics and density estimation.}
Fruin~\cite{Fruin71} established the Level of Service framework; the LoS-E boundary at $\rho\approx 4$\,ped/m$^2$ marks the density above which pedestrians lose the freedom to choose their walking direction, motivating the sigmoid centre we adopt for ORCA fade-out. Helbing et al.~\cite{Helbing07} characterised crowd turbulence at sustained high densities. Steffen and Seyfried~\cite{Steffen10} and Tordeux et al.~\cite{Tordeux15} established Voronoi tessellation as a higher-fidelity local density estimator than grid counting, motivating its use here.

\section{\uppercase{Methodology}}
\label{sec:framework}

We describe (i) the density-modulated additive acceleration formulation that defines steering configurations C1--C4, (ii) the Voronoi-based density estimator driving the blending weight, and (iii) phenomenological calibration.

\subsection{Hybrid Steering Model}
\label{sec:hybrid}

Each agent~$i$ has position~$\mathbf{x}_i$, velocity~$\mathbf{v}_i$, goal~$\mathbf{g}_i$, radius~$r_i$, desired speed~$v_i^0$, mass~$m_i$, and relaxation time~$\tau_i$. The per-agent steering is an \emph{additive blend} of accelerations in which only the ORCA term is density-weighted:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hybrid}
    \mathbf{a}_i = \mathbf{a}_i^{\text{des}} + \mathbf{a}_i^{\text{SFM}} + \mathbf{a}_i^{\text{TTC}} + w_o(\rho_i)\,\mathbf{a}_i^{\text{ORCA}} + \mathbf{a}_i^{\text{wall}}
\end{equation}
where $w_o(\rho_i)\in[0,1]$ fades ORCA out as local density rises (Eq.~\ref{eq:sigmoid}); the desired, SFM, TTC, and wall components are unconditional. Per-component accelerations are obtained as $\mathbf{F}/m_i$ from the force expressions below. The four steering configurations are C1 (SFM only), C2 (SFM+TTC), C3 (SFM+ORCA), and C4 (full blend). Algorithm~\ref{alg:step} summarises one timestep.

\textbf{Desired force.}
The goal-seeking force drives agents toward their destination with a density-dependent speed reduction following the Weidmann~\cite{Weidmann93} fundamental diagram:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:desired}
    \mathbf{F}_i^{\text{des}} = \frac{m_i}{\tau_i}\left(v_i^{\text{eff}}(\rho_i)\,\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i - \mathbf{v}_i\right),\ v_i^{\text{eff}} = v_i^0\bigl(1 - e^{-\gamma(1/\rho_i - 1/\rho_{\max})}\bigr)
\end{equation}

\textbf{Social Force Model.}
Agent-agent interaction follows Helbing et al.~\cite{Helbing00}:
\begin{multline}\label{eq:sfm}
    \mathbf{f}_{ij} = A\,e^{(r_{ij} - d_{ij})/B}\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij} + k\,g(r_{ij} - d_{ij})\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij} \\
    + \kappa\,g(r_{ij} - d_{ij})\,(\Delta\mathbf{v}_{ji}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{t}}_{ij})\,\hat{\mathbf{t}}_{ij}
\end{multline}
with $r_{ij}=r_i+r_j$, $d_{ij}=\|\mathbf{x}_i-\mathbf{x}_j\|$, $g(x)=\max(0,x)$, and parameters in Table~\ref{tab:params}.

\textbf{Time-to-collision force.}
Following Karamouzas et al.~\cite{Karamouzas14}, agents apply repulsive forces that decay exponentially with time-to-collision $\tau_{ij}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ttc}
    F_{\text{TTC}} = \frac{k_{\text{ttc}}\,e^{-\tau_{ij}/\tau_0}}{\tau_{ij}^2},\ \tau_{ij} = \frac{-b - \sqrt{b^2-ac}}{a}
\end{equation}

\textbf{ORCA.}
At low density, ORCA~\cite{VandenBerg11} constructs half-plane velocity constraints from each neighbour and solves a 2D linear program for the velocity closest to $\mathbf{v}_i^{\text{pref}}=v_i^0\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i$. The resulting velocity is converted to a force: $\mathbf{F}_i^{\text{ORCA}} = m_i(\mathbf{v}_i^{\text{ORCA}}-\mathbf{v}_i)/\tau_{\text{ORCA}}$ with $\tau_{\text{ORCA}}=0.5$\,s, following Guy et al.~\cite{Guy10}. This conversion is an engineering choice that does not inherit ORCA's formal collision-free guarantee, which is a property of the LP-selected velocity rather than of any force obtained by relaxing toward it over a finite timescale.

\textbf{Density-dependent weighting.}
The ORCA contribution is modulated by a sigmoid of local density:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sigmoid}
    w_o(\rho) = 1 - \sigma(\rho;\,4.0,\,2.0),\ \sigma(x;x_0,k)=\tfrac{1}{1+e^{-k(x-x_0)}}
\end{equation}
The fade centre $\rho_0=4.0$\,ped/m$^2$ is the Fruin LoS-E boundary~\cite{Fruin71}, above which pedestrians lose the freedom to choose walking direction; it is \emph{not} fit to the empirical force-magnitude crossover $|\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{SFM}}|=|\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{ORCA}}|$, which occurs near $\rho\approx 0.07$\,ped/m$^2$ (Section~\ref{sec:force-diag}) and measures a different quantity (raw force-magnitude equality versus paradigm applicability). The slope $k=2.0$ gives a smooth transition over approximately $\pm 2$\,ped/m$^2$ around $\rho_0$. Only the ORCA term is density-modulated; SFM and TTC contribute unconditionally because they provide body-compression / friction and anticipatory decay that ORCA does not model.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{One simulation timestep (density-modulated additive steering).}\label{alg:step}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Build KDTree from active positions
\State $\mathcal{N}_i \leftarrow$ neighbours within $R=3.0$\,m
\State $\rho_i \leftarrow$ Voronoi cell density (Sec.~\ref{sec:density})
\State $w_o(\rho_i) \leftarrow 1 - \sigma(\rho_i;\,4.0,\,2.0)$
\State $\mathbf{a}_i^{\text{des}} \leftarrow (v_i^{\text{eff}}(\rho_i)\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i - \mathbf{v}_i)/\tau_i$
\State $\mathbf{a}_i^{\text{SFM}} \leftarrow (1/m_i)\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\mathbf{f}_{ij}$ \Comment{Eq.~\ref{eq:sfm}}
\State $\mathbf{a}_i^{\text{TTC}} \leftarrow (1/m_i)\sum_{j\in\mathcal{N}_i}\mathbf{F}_{\text{TTC}}$ \Comment{Eq.~\ref{eq:ttc}}
\State $\mathbf{v}_i^{\text{ORCA}} \leftarrow$ solve 2D LP from half-plane constraints
\State $\mathbf{a}_i^{\text{ORCA}} \leftarrow (\mathbf{v}_i^{\text{ORCA}}-\mathbf{v}_i)/\tau_{\text{ORCA}}$
\State $\mathbf{a}_i \leftarrow \mathbf{a}_i^{\text{des}} + \mathbf{a}_i^{\text{SFM}} + \mathbf{a}_i^{\text{TTC}} + w_o\,\mathbf{a}_i^{\text{ORCA}} + \mathbf{a}_i^{\text{wall}}$
\State $\mathbf{v}_i \leftarrow \mathbf{v}_i + \mathbf{a}_i\Delta t$; $\mathbf{x}_i \leftarrow \mathbf{x}_i + \mathbf{v}_i\Delta t$
\State Clamp $\|\mathbf{v}_i\|\le 2 v_i^0$; deactivate agents at goal
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Local Density via Voronoi Tessellation}
\label{sec:density}

The blending weight is driven by a per-agent local density estimate $\rho_V(i) = 1/A_i^{\text{Vor}}$, where $A_i^{\text{Vor}}$ is the Voronoi cell area clipped to the scenario domain. Boundary cells are stabilised with the mirror-point construction of Mullick et al.~\cite{Mullick24}. Voronoi density is preferred over fixed-disk grid counting because grid averaging smooths over local hotspots at narrow throats that drive paradigm switching~\cite{Steffen10}.

\subsection{Phenomenological Calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}

The Weidmann coefficients $(\gamma,\rho_{\max})$ that drive the density-dependent speed reduction in Eq.~\eqref{eq:desired} are primarily responsible for any mismatch between simulated and empirical fundamental diagrams. We fit $\boldsymbol{\phi}=(\gamma,\rho_{\max})$ with SFM social-repulsion $A$ and $B$ held fixed at textbook Helbing et al.~\cite{Helbing00} values. Given binned FZJ measurements $\{(\rho_k,v_k^{\text{emp}})\}$ (10 bins, $\rho\in[0.5,5.0]$\,ped/m$^2$, 3 seeds per bin), we minimise
\begin{equation}\label{eq:calibration}
\boldsymbol{\phi}^* = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\bigl(v_k^{\text{sim}}(\boldsymbol{\phi})-v_k^{\text{emp}}\bigr)^2}
\end{equation}
via Nelder--Mead from the textbook initial point with tolerances $x_{\text{atol}}{=}10^{-3}$, $f_{\text{atol}}{=}10^{-4}$. Optimisation terminates at the seed-noise floor (evaluations 44--53 oscillate within $\pm 0.003$\,m/s) rather than at formal tolerance convergence; the full protocol and per-evaluation trace are persisted as \texttt{calibration\_alight\_result.json}.

\textbf{Metric definitions.}
A \emph{contact-overlap} is counted each timestep in which two agents satisfy $d_{ij}<r_i+r_j$; the metric is timestep-dependent (halving $\Delta t$ approximately doubles the per-run sum) and we use it for relative comparison at fixed $\Delta t=0.01$\,s. As a $\Delta t$-independent supplement we report \emph{contact-events} (maximal runs of consecutive overlap timesteps per pair; Section~\ref{sec:bottleneck-evac}). Robustness to $\Delta t\in\{0.005,0.01,0.02\}$\,s is verified; the C1\,$>$\,C4 rank ordering is preserved at all three values. For scenarios that do not fully evacuate within the time window we report \emph{throughput}; deadlock success is reported as a binomial proportion with 95\% Wilson CI.

\section{\uppercase{Experiments and Results}}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate C1 (SFM only), C2 (SFM+TTC), C3 (SFM+ORCA), and C4 (full hybrid). All configurations share parameters from Table~\ref{tab:params}. Agents are heterogeneous with speed drawn from $\mathcal{N}(1.34,0.26)$ and radius from $\mathcal{N}(0.25,0.03)$. ORCA's per-agent linear program makes C3/C4 approximately 40$\times$ slower than C1/C2 at 200 agents, imposing a budget of $n=25$ replications per cell for ablation experiments and for C3/C4 of the FD; C1/C2 of the FD use $n=30$ from an earlier run. Statistical tests are negative-binomial GLM for contact-overlap counts, Cox proportional hazards for deadlock survival (Fisher's exact for the C2 complete-separation case), and linear mixed models with random intercept by seed for continuous metrics. Holm--\v{S}id\'ak correction is applied across pairwise configuration comparisons within each scenario\,$\times$\,metric combination.

\subsection{FD Calibration}
\label{sec:fd-validation}

We measure the speed--density relationship in a periodic corridor (18\,m\,$\times$\,5\,m) matching the FZJ unidirectional corridor geometry~\cite{FZJ13a}. Density is controlled by agent count after 300 warmup steps; mean speed is recorded over 500 measurement steps.

\textbf{The FD is insensitive to steering.}
Figure~\ref{fig:fd} overlays all four configurations on 4776 empirical FZJ points; the curves are nearly indistinguishable --- the FD is determined by the Weidmann speed-reduction term, not by steering choice. The calibration below aligns the desired-force speed--density curve to empirical data and does \emph{not} validate the steering paradigm comparison.

With textbook parameters, the simulated curve sits above empirical FZJ data at mid-density, giving an in-sample RMSE of 0.235\,m/s. The 2-parameter $(\gamma,\rho_{\max})$ single-start Nelder--Mead fit of Section~\ref{sec:calibration} (with $A$, $B$ fixed) reduces the in-sample FD RMSE to 0.099\,m/s. The calibrated values are $\gamma^*=0.833$ and $\rho_{\max}^*=5.98$.

\subsection{Held-Out OOD Validation}
\label{sec:ood}

To assess out-of-distribution generalisation, we compare the calibrated model's bottleneck flow rates against FZJ empirical bottleneck measurements at five exit widths (Table~\ref{tab:ood}). The model was calibrated exclusively on unidirectional corridor data; the bottleneck geometry was unseen during fitting. Simulated flow rates systematically underestimate empirical values by 26.5\% to 42.9\%. The gap motivates the diagnostic ablation approach: rather than claiming the calibrated model generalises across geometries, we characterise where each steering paradigm fails and use the ablation to locate the throughput deficit.

\subsection{Density-Adaptive Gate}
\label{sec:force-diag}

The density-adaptive gate $w_o(\rho)=1-\sigma(\rho;\rho_0,k)$ transitions across $\rho\in[2.9,5.1]$\,ped/m$^2$, centred at $\rho_0=4.0$. Figure~\ref{fig:force} plots the per-component force magnitudes versus local Voronoi density from 55{,}727 agent-timestep samples (C4, $w=1.0$\,m, $n=5$ seeds). Two features are visible. First, the SFM and ORCA magnitudes cross near $\rho\approx 0.07$\,ped/m$^2$ (dotted), essentially first-contact density. Second, all observed densities sit below $\rho\approx 1.56$ (99.9th pct), orders of magnitude below the $\rho_0=4.0$ transition band (dashed); the gate's transition region is never exercised and the sigmoid functions as a fixed blend ($w_o\approx 1$ throughout) rather than as an adaptive transition in these scenarios. The empirical force-magnitude crossover and the Fruin LoS-E gate centre carry different meanings (raw force-magnitude equality versus paradigm applicability), so we retain the literature-motivated value for $\rho_0$. Scenarios that sustain densities in the transition-band regime, together with a sensitivity sweep over $(\rho_0,k)$, are future work.

\subsection{Zone-Decomposed Ablation}
\label{sec:ablation}

We compare C1--C4 across three scenarios with $n=25$ per cell. Bottleneck uses 50 agents through variable exit widths; bidirectional uses $100+100$ counter-flowing agents; crossing uses $100+100$ agents at a 90\textdegree{} intersection. Bottleneck contact-overlaps are classified by zone relative to the exit at $x=10$\,m: upstream ($x<8$), throat ($8\le x\le 10.5$), and downstream ($x>10.5$).

\textbf{Bottleneck contact-overlap reduction concentrates upstream, not at the throat.}
At $w=1.0$\,m, the 31\% C1\,$\to$\,C4 reduction in total bottleneck contact-overlaps (NB GLM IRR $=0.695$, 95\% CI $[0.628,0.771]$, $p_{\mathrm{HS}}<0.001$) originates almost entirely upstream: 96\% of the absolute reduction comes from the queue-formation zone (C1 $41.8\pm 15.8$; C4 $29.3\pm 12.2$), while the throat contributes only 4\% and carries $<1$ overlap per run in either configuration (Table~\ref{tab:zonal}, Figure~\ref{fig:zonal}). The mechanism is TTC's anticipatory deceleration preventing rear-end overlaps during queue compression, not relief at the exit throat. The same upstream-dominant pattern holds at $w=0.8$\,m (C1 $320.9\to$ C4 $215.0$).

\textbf{Crossing scenario.}
At the 90\textdegree{} intersection, SFM-only (C1) agents reach force-balance standoffs, with only 5.9 of 200 agents exiting in the 60\,s window. Absolute throughput remains low; the 90\textdegree{} intersection is pathological for force-balance models, and this result should be read as relative mitigation within a scenario where C1 performs poorly.

\textbf{Bidirectional scenario.}
In counter-flowing groups ($n=25$), the full hybrid increases mean speed relative to C1 (LMM coef.\ $+0.074$\,m/s, 95\% CI $[0.048,0.100]$, $p_{\mathrm{HS}}<10^{-6}$). Throughput is not significantly different from C1 (LMM coef.\ $+4.76$ exits in 60\,s; $p_{\mathrm{HS}}=0.60$); we frame this as comparable throughput with a faster steady-state flow. ORCA alone (C3) shows slightly reduced throughput (coef.\ $-5.0$; $p_{\mathrm{HS}}=0.60$); the combination (C4) recovers it, suggesting TTC's contact-overlap reduction prevents the counterflow gridlock that pure ORCA yielding would otherwise create.

\textbf{Oracle baseline.}
To quantify the blend's benefit over naive paradigm selection, we define an oracle as the per-scenario best single paradigm. Across eight scenario--metric pairs, C4 improves over the oracle in 5 cases, matches within 1\% in 2, and underperforms in 1 by 1.4\% (paired Wilcoxon across seeds). The largest oracle gap is $+7.7\%$ on $w=0.8$\,m deadlock completion (C4 56\% vs.\ oracle C3 52\%); the underperformance is $+1.4\%$ evacuation time at $w=3.6$\,m, where exit throughput is already saturated.

\subsection{Bottleneck Deadlock and Mechanism}
\label{sec:bottleneck-evac}

Table~\ref{tab:bottleneck} reports mean evacuation times ($n=25$) across five exit widths, with C1 vs.\ C4 differences tested by Holm--\v{S}id\'ak-corrected LMM.

\textbf{Normal exits (1.0--3.6\,m).} The hybrid produces a statistically significant evacuation-time reduction at narrow exits: 9.3\% at $w=1.0$\,m and 7.4\% at $w=1.2$\,m, a marginal 2.9\% at $w=1.8$\,m, and converges at $w\ge 2.4$\,m (Figure~\ref{fig:evac_width}).

\subsection{External Simulator Comparison}
\label{sec:external}

We compared C1 and C4 against JuPedSim v1.3.2 at the $w=1.0$\,m bottleneck (50 agents, $n=25$, tool-default parameters) as a qualitative sanity check --- parameters are not harmonised across the two simulators (desired speed, agent radius, relaxation time all differ), so this is not an external validation. JuPedSim's CollisionFreeSpeed (CFS) model evacuates in $33.6\pm 1.2$\,s, 34\% faster than our C4; JuPedSim SocialForce (SF), the closest analogue to our C1, exits only $31.3\pm 2.3$ of 50 agents in the 60\,s window (0/25 complete) compared to 50/50 for our C1. The parameter gap produces opposite-direction deviations, illustrating that identical parameter differences can hurt or help depending on the paradigm; the qualitative ordering (velocity-based models evacuate faster than force-based models at moderate bottleneck widths) is consistent across both frameworks, supporting the interpretation that this is a property of the steering paradigms rather than an implementation artefact. JuPedSim's C++ core runs approximately 39$\times$ faster than our pure-Python implementation (Table~\ref{tab:external}).

\subsection{Computational Performance}
\label{sec:scaling-eval}

All timings measured on Apple M1, 8\,GB RAM, Python 3.13.12 (single core). Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} shows per-step timing. \section{\uppercase{Discussion}}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Why each component matters, and why each can also harm.}
TTC and ORCA serve distinct, complementary roles --- and each can be harmful in the wrong regime. TTC-enabled configurations reduce contact-overlaps by 30\% (IRR $=0.695$, $p_{\mathrm{HS}}<0.001$), concentrated upstream, and at 90\textdegree{} crossings TTC's predictive evasion dissolves force-balance standoffs to roughly triple throughput; yet TTC alone does not break symmetric arches (C2: 0/25 at $w=0.8$\,m). ORCA-enabled configurations resolve the arch in 52--56\% of trials while neither C1 nor C2 resolves it at all; yet ORCA alone depresses counterflow throughput (C3 coef.\ $-5.0$ exits in 60\,s relative to C1), because its collision-free velocity selection makes agents yield to counterflow neighbours and creates gridlock. The full hybrid (C4) recovers throughput because TTC's contact-overlap reduction prevents the gridlock that pure ORCA yielding would cause. The $\sigma$-sweep locates the noise threshold for deadlock resolution at $\sigma_{50}=0.049$\,m/s (95\% CI $[0.044,0.053]$); passive Gaussian noise at $\sigma\approx\sigma_{50}$ recovers ORCA's effect within sampling resolution, and within $n=25$ the residual contribution of ORCA's velocity-space optimisation beyond symmetry-breaking is not separable from noise. As a $\Delta t$-independent supplement, mean contact-event counts at $w=1.0$\,m are 18.5 for C1 versus 15.3 for C4, preserving the direction of the overlap-count result. Both negative findings --- TTC harming deadlock, ORCA harming counterflow --- show that na\"ive always-on superposition can be worse than any single paradigm. This motivates the density-gated architecture: each component activates where it helps and fades where it would hurt.

\textbf{Oracle baseline.}
Across eight scenario--metric pairs, the full blend matches or slightly exceeds the best single paradigm in seven, without runtime scenario classification. The single $+1.4\%$ underperformance is at $w=3.6$\,m where throughput is saturated; no scenario shows C4 significantly worse than the best single paradigm.

\textbf{Phenomenological calibration.}
The 2-parameter $(\gamma,\rho_{\max})$ Nelder--Mead fit reduces in-sample FD RMSE by 57.7\%, but because Figure~\ref{fig:fd} shows the FD is identical across C1--C4, this fit does \emph{not} validate the steering paradigm comparison; it is reported as a phenomenological alignment of the speed--density curve.

\textbf{What this paper does not claim.}
(i) The additive blend is a mitigation probe, not a deployable controller; we make no production-readiness claim. (ii) Calibration and ablation results are validated at the FD and flow-rate level but not at the steering-trajectory level. (iii) The scaling analysis characterises computational cost on a single workstation rather than demonstrating real-time deployment. (iv) The held-out bottleneck bias of $-26\%$ to $-43\%$ indicates the framework does not generalise quantitatively across geometries. (v) The density-adaptive gate is not exercised in its intended transition regime in any of the three scenarios reported --- observed densities sit below the transition band, so the gate functions as a near-fixed blend rather than an adaptive switch. (vi) At $n=25$ the $C_1+\varepsilon$ experiment cannot separate passive Gaussian noise from ORCA's LP-based velocity selection; larger-$n$ separation is future work.

\textbf{Limitations.}
Replication counts of $n=25$ reflect a fixed compute budget. The calibration is in-sample on FZJ corridor data; the held-out OOD gap (26--43\% bottleneck flow underestimation) indicates the calibrated model does not generalise across geometries. The per-paradigm OOD decomposition (Table~\ref{tab:ood-per-paradigm}) covers 2 of 5 held-out widths. The force-magnitude crossover $\rho\approx 0.07$\,ped/m$^2$ is offset from the literature-motivated $\rho_0=4.0$ because the two quantities carry different meanings; we retained the Fruin value without a sensitivity sweep over $(\rho_0,k)$, which is deferred along with experiments that traverse the transition band. The external simulator comparison uses tool-default parameters rather than a harmonised comparison. The sigmoid is not hysteretic; we observed no oscillation artefacts but recommend hysteresis for deployments at densities sustained near $\rho_0$.

\section{\uppercase{Conclusions}}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a diagnostic zone-decomposed ablation of three pedestrian steering paradigms (SFM, TTC, ORCA) identifying two paradigm-specific failure modes; a mechanism-level deadlock study via the $C_1+\varepsilon$ control; and a density-modulated additive blend as a mitigation probe. Held-out FZJ bottleneck flow rates are systematically underestimated by 26--43\%, indicating the calibration generalises within-corridor but not across-geometry. FD calibration aligns the speed--density relation (57.7\% in-sample RMSE reduction) but does not validate steering --- the FD is identical across C1--C4. The noise-threshold control ($\sigma_{50}\approx 0.049$\,m/s, 95\% CI $[0.044,0.053]$) shows that at $n=25$, passive Gaussian noise is indistinguishable from ORCA's LP-based velocity selection as a deadlock-breaking mechanism. Code, data, and reproduction scripts are released with the paper.

Future work targets (i) sensitivity analysis of the density gate and steering hyperparameters ($\rho_0$, $k$, $A$, $B$), (ii) trajectory-level validation on additional geometries (ETH--UCY, further FZJ scenarios), (iii) higher-density scenarios that exercise the adaptive transition region, (iv) a larger-$n$ $\sigma$-sweep to separate passive-noise symmetry-breaking from ORCA's velocity-space optimisation, (v) C/C++ acceleration of the ORCA LP to unlock real-time scale, and (vi) a full 4-parameter multi-restart calibration of $(\gamma, \rho_{\max}, A, B)$. A natural direction for the mitigation-probe architecture is evaluation as a component of a digital-twin pipeline once trajectory-level validation is in place; that evaluation is out of scope here.
