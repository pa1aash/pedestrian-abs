\keywords{Crowd Simulation, Hybrid Steering, Social Force Model, Collision Avoidance, Crowd Safety, Empirical Calibration.}

\abstract{No single pedestrian steering model performs well across the full density spectrum. We present a density-adaptive hybrid framework that continuously blends Social Force Model contact forces, time-to-collision anticipation, and ORCA velocity optimization using sigmoid functions of local density, with thresholds grounded in the Fruin Level-of-Service and Helbing crowd-turbulence literature. Automated Nelder-Mead calibration of Weidmann speed-density parameters against 4776 FZJ empirical data points reduces the fundamental-diagram RMSE by 60.5\%. Ablation experiments ($n = 25$, Welch's $t$-test) show that the hybrid achieves 6--10\% faster evacuation at narrow bottlenecks ($p < 0.01$) and 29--33\% fewer collisions across all exit widths; at 90\textdegree{} crossings, TTC-enabled configurations triple throughput ($p < 0.001$); at 0.8\,m exits, ORCA-enabled configurations resolve arching deadlocks in 52--56\% of trials where SFM alone succeeds in 4\%. Notably, TTC alone \emph{worsens} deadlocks (0/25), revealing that the two mechanisms serve complementary rather than redundant roles. An exploratory crush regime suggests $\sim$35\% collision reduction at extreme densities. The calibrated framework is suitable for integration into digital twin workflows.}

\section{\uppercase{Introduction}}
\label{sec:introduction}

Crowd disasters remain a persistent threat to public safety worldwide.
The 2022 Itaewon crowd crush in Seoul killed 159 people in a narrow alley during Halloween celebrations~\cite{Lee24}, while the 2010 Love Parade disaster in Duisburg resulted in 21 deaths at a tunnel bottleneck~\cite{Helbing12}.
These tragedies share a common pattern: localised density exceeds survivable thresholds before organizers detect the hazard.
Predictive tools that can anticipate dangerous density buildups and evaluate mitigation strategies \emph{before} an event are therefore critical for crowd safety management.

Pedestrian crowd simulation has matured considerably since the seminal Social Force Model (SFM) of Helbing and Moln\'{a}r~\cite{Helbing95}.
Force-based approaches~\cite{Helbing00}, velocity-obstacle methods~\cite{Fiorini98,VandenBerg11}, and anticipatory models based on time-to-collision (TTC)~\cite{Karamouzas14} each capture different aspects of pedestrian behaviour.
However, no single paradigm performs well across the full density spectrum: the SFM excels at contact-rich high-density regimes but produces unrealistic paths at low density, while ORCA~\cite{VandenBerg11} yields smooth collision-free trajectories in sparse crowds but cannot model the physical compression forces that dominate crush scenarios.
Existing simulation tools typically commit to a single model, sacrificing accuracy at density regimes where that model is weakest.

The Digital Twin (DT) concept~\cite{Grieves17} provides a natural deployment context for such a hybrid simulator: a virtual replica of a physical venue, calibrated against empirical data and capable of what-if analysis.
Recent crowd DT systems for railway stations~\cite{Gatta24} and evacuation~\cite{Lin25} embed single simulation models; a density-adaptive hybrid could improve the fidelity of DT-based safety assessment.

This paper presents a density-adaptive hybrid crowd simulation framework designed for integration into digital twin workflows.
Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{density-adaptive hybrid steering model} that continuously blends SFM, TTC, and ORCA forces using sigmoid activation functions of local density, with thresholds grounded in the Fruin Level-of-Service~\cite{Fruin71} and Helbing crowd-turbulence~\cite{Helbing07} literature.
    \item \textbf{Empirical calibration and cross-scenario evaluation}: automated Nelder-Mead fitting of Weidmann speed-density parameters against FZJ data (60.5\% RMSE reduction), with ablation experiments across bottleneck, bidirectional, and crossing scenarios ($n = 25$, Welch's $t$-test).
    \item \textbf{Safety-oriented extensions} including multi-method density estimation (grid, Voronoi, KDE) with a composite risk metric, derivative-free barrier optimization, and an exploratory density-dependent crush regime.
\end{enumerate}

We validate the hybrid model against 4776 empirical data points from the FZJ Pedestrian Dynamics Data Archive~\cite{FZJ13a}, demonstrating that calibrated parameters reduce the speed-density RMSE by 60.5\%.
Ablation experiments across bottleneck, bidirectional, and crossing scenarios quantify the complementary contribution of each steering component.
The calibrated framework is designed for integration into digital twin workflows~\cite{Grieves17,Lin25}, providing the simulation fidelity needed for offline what-if crowd safety analysis.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows.
Section~2 reviews related work in pedestrian simulation, crowd safety metrics, and digital twins.
Section~3 presents the framework architecture including the hybrid steering model, density estimation, and optimization components.
Section~4 describes the experimental setup and results.
Section~5 discusses findings and limitations.
Section~6 concludes with future directions.

\section{\uppercase{Related Work}}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Force-Based and Velocity-Based Models}

The Social Force Model (SFM) introduced by Helbing and Moln\'{a}r~\cite{Helbing95} represents pedestrians as particles subject to desired, social repulsive, and contact forces.
The extended model~\cite{Helbing00} added body compression and sliding friction forces that activate upon physical contact, enabling simulation of dense crowds and panic scenarios.
Reynolds~\cite{Reynolds99} proposed steering behaviours for autonomous agents, while Fiorini and Shiller~\cite{Fiorini98} formalized velocity obstacles (VO) for collision avoidance.
Van den Berg et al.~\cite{VandenBerg11} introduced Optimal Reciprocal Collision Avoidance (ORCA), which constructs half-plane velocity constraints and solves a linear program to find the optimal collision-free velocity.
Karamouzas et al.~\cite{Karamouzas14} demonstrated that pedestrians respond to anticipated time-to-collision with an inverse power-law force, providing an anticipatory steering mechanism.
Guy et al.~\cite{Guy10} developed PLEdestrians, optimizing agent parameters to match real pedestrian trajectories.

These models are typically deployed individually. Force-based approaches handle contact-rich scenarios well but produce oscillatory paths at low density; velocity-based methods yield smooth trajectories but cannot represent physical compression. While each paradigm has been extensively studied in isolation, their continuous density-weighted combination within a single agent remains largely unexplored.

\subsection{Hybrid and Multi-Regime Models}

Several authors have explored combining simulation paradigms.
Van Toll et al.~\cite{VanToll21} proposed SPH-based crowd simulation that transitions between microscopic and macroscopic scales.
Moussa\"{\i}d et al.~\cite{Moussaid11} developed heuristic-based models calibrated to empirical data from bottleneck experiments, though these remain single-paradigm.
Cristiani et al.~\cite{Cristiani23} introduced a multi-scale simulator handling all density regimes through model switching.
Gaudou et al.~\cite{Gaudou24} surveyed agent-based crowd simulation approaches, identifying the need for models that adapt their resolution to local conditions.

Existing hybrids typically combine at most two paradigms (e.g., macro-micro coupling) and use discrete switching rather than continuous blending. Our work differs in applying continuous density-weighted sigmoids to blend SFM contact forces, TTC anticipation, and ORCA velocity optimization within each agent at every timestep.

\subsection{Crowd Safety Metrics and Density Estimation}

Fruin~\cite{Fruin71} established the Level of Service framework relating pedestrian density to comfort and safety.
Helbing et al.~\cite{Helbing07} defined crowd pressure as the product of density and velocity variance, providing a metric sensitive to the turbulent conditions preceding crowd crushes.
Steffen and Seyfried~\cite{Steffen10} demonstrated that Voronoi tessellation provides more accurate local density estimates than grid-based counting in heterogeneous crowds.
Tordeux et al.~\cite{Tordeux15} compared density estimation methods and found that the choice of method significantly affects measured fundamental diagrams.
Mullick et al.~\cite{Mullick24} extended Voronoi density to handle boundary effects through mirror-point techniques.
Feliciani et al.~\cite{Feliciani20} proposed congestion and danger metrics based on density gradients, arguing that spatial variation in density is a stronger predictor of incidents than density alone.

While individual density estimators have been compared~\cite{Tordeux15}, their combination into a composite risk metric integrating density magnitude, crowd pressure, and density gradients has received limited attention.

\subsection{Digital Twins for Crowd Management}

Grieves and Vickers~\cite{Grieves17} formalized the Digital Twin concept as a virtual replica connected to its physical counterpart through data exchange.
Croatti and Ricci~\cite{Croatti26} proposed an agent-based architecture for crowd digital twins (CDT) supporting real-time monitoring and what-if analysis.
Gatta et al.~\cite{Gatta24} developed a railway station CDT integrating pedestrian simulation with IoT sensor data.
Lin et al.~\cite{Lin25} surveyed digital twin applications for emergency evacuation, identifying crowd simulation fidelity as the primary bottleneck.

Published CDT systems typically embed a single simulation model. Our framework provides the density-adaptive hybrid steering and empirical calibration needed to improve the simulation fidelity of such systems.

\section{\uppercase{Framework Architecture}}
\label{sec:framework}

The proposed framework consists of three core components: (1)~a hybrid steering model that blends force-based and velocity-based paradigms, (2)~multi-method density estimation with composite risk assessment, and (3)~derivative-free barrier optimization. The framework additionally includes a density-dependent crush regime for extreme-density scenarios (Section~\ref{sec:crush}). We describe each in turn.

\subsection{Hybrid Steering Model}
\label{sec:hybrid}

Each agent~$i$ is characterized by position~$\mathbf{x}_i$, velocity~$\mathbf{v}_i$, goal~$\mathbf{g}_i$, radius~$r_i$, desired speed~$v_i^0$, mass~$m_i$, and relaxation time~$\tau_i$.
The total force on agent~$i$ is:
\begin{multline}\label{eq:hybrid}
    \mathbf{F}_i = (1 - w_c)\,\mathbf{F}_i^{\text{des}} + \mathbf{F}_i^{\text{SFM}} + \mathbf{F}_i^{\text{TTC}} \\
    + w_o\,\mathbf{F}_i^{\text{ORCA}} + w_c\,\mathbf{F}_i^{\text{crush}} + \mathbf{F}_i^{\text{wall}}
\end{multline}
where $w_o(\rho_i)$ and $w_c(\rho_i)$ are density-dependent weights controlling the contribution of ORCA and crush forces respectively.

\textbf{Desired Force.}
The goal-seeking force drives agents toward their destination with a density-dependent speed reduction following the Weidmann fundamental diagram~\cite{Weidmann93}:
\begin{gather}\label{eq:desired}
    \mathbf{F}_i^{\text{des}} = \frac{m_i}{\tau_i}\left(v_i^{\text{eff}}(\rho_i)\,\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i - \mathbf{v}_i\right) \\
    v_i^{\text{eff}} = v_i^0\left(1 - e^{-\gamma(1/\rho_i - 1/\rho_{\max})}\right) \notag
\end{gather}
with $\gamma = 1.913$ and $\rho_{\max} = 5.4$~ped/m$^2$.

\textbf{Social Force Model.}
Agent-agent interaction follows Helbing et al.~\cite{Helbing00} with social repulsion, body compression, and sliding friction:
\begin{multline}\label{eq:sfm}
    \mathbf{f}_{ij} = A\,e^{(r_{ij} - d_{ij})/B}\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij} + k\,g(r_{ij} - d_{ij})\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij} \\
    + \kappa\,g(r_{ij} - d_{ij})\,(\Delta\mathbf{v}_{ji} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{t}}_{ij})\,\hat{\mathbf{t}}_{ij}
\end{multline}
where $r_{ij} = r_i + r_j$ is the sum of radii, $d_{ij} = \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\|$ the distance, $g(x) = \max(0, x)$, and parameters $A = 2000$~N, $B = 0.08$~m, $k = 1.2 \times 10^5$~kg/s$^2$, $\kappa = 2.4 \times 10^5$~kg/(m$\cdot$s).

\textbf{Time-to-Collision Force.}
Following Karamouzas et al.~\cite{Karamouzas14}, agents anticipate collisions and apply repulsive forces that decay exponentially with time-to-collision~$\tau_{ij}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ttc}
    F_{\text{TTC}} = \frac{k_{\text{ttc}}\,e^{-\tau_{ij}/\tau_0}}{\tau_{ij}^2}, \quad
    \tau_{ij} = \frac{-b - \sqrt{b^2 - ac}}{a}
\end{equation}
with $k_{\text{ttc}} = 1.5$, $\tau_0 = 3.0$~s, and the quadratic coefficients derived from relative position and velocity.

\textbf{ORCA.}
At low density, ORCA~\cite{VandenBerg11} constructs half-plane velocity constraints from each neighbour and solves a 2D linear program to find the velocity closest to the preferred velocity while satisfying all constraints. The resulting velocity is converted to a force: $\mathbf{F}_i^{\text{ORCA}} = m_i(\mathbf{v}_i^{\text{ORCA}} - \mathbf{v}_i) / \tau_{\text{ORCA}}$, preserving the collision-free velocity target while enabling smooth blending with force-based components~\cite{Guy10}.

\textbf{Density-Dependent Weighting.}
The ORCA and crush contributions are modulated by sigmoid functions of local density:
\begin{gather}\label{eq:sigmoid}
    w_o(\rho) = 1 - \sigma(\rho;\, 4.0,\, 2.0), \;\;
    w_c(\rho) = \sigma(\rho;\, 5.5,\, 3.0) \\
    \sigma(x; x_0, k) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-k(x - x_0)}} \notag
\end{gather}
The ORCA fade centre $\rho_0 = 4.0$~ped/m\textsuperscript{2} corresponds to Fruin's Level-of-Service~E boundary~\cite{Fruin71}, above which pedestrians lose the ability to freely choose their walking direction --- the regime where ORCA's free-navigation assumption breaks down.
The crush activation centre $\rho_{\text{crit}} = 5.5$~ped/m\textsuperscript{2} aligns with the critical density identified by Helbing et al.~\cite{Helbing07} at which crowd pressure produces involuntary body contact and the onset of ``crowd turbulence.''
The slope parameters ($k = 2.0$ and $3.0$) produce smooth transitions spanning approximately $\pm 2$~ped/m\textsuperscript{2} around each centre, avoiding discontinuous model switching.
At low density ($\rho < 2$), ORCA dominates ($w_o \approx 1$); at high density ($\rho > 6$), crush forces take over ($w_c \approx 1$).
The SFM contributes at all densities: at high density it provides body-compression and friction forces that neither TTC nor ORCA model; at low density its social-repulsion term provides personal-space maintenance complementary to ORCA's geometric avoidance.

\subsection{Density Estimation and Composite Risk}
\label{sec:density}

We estimate local density using three complementary methods.
\textbf{Grid counting} computes $\rho_G(i) = |\{j : \|\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i\| < R\}| / (\pi R^2)$ with $R = 2.0$~m.
\textbf{Voronoi tessellation}~\cite{Steffen10} assigns $\rho_V(i) = 1 / A_i^{\text{Vor}}$ where $A_i^{\text{Vor}}$ is the clipped Voronoi cell area, using mirror points for boundary handling~\cite{Mullick24}.
\textbf{Kernel density estimation} uses a Gaussian kernel with bandwidth $h = 1.0$.

The composite risk metric~\cite{Feliciani20,Helbing07} combines density, crowd pressure, and density gradient:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:risk}
    R_i = \frac{\hat{\rho}_i}{\rho_{\text{ref}}} \left(1 + \frac{P_i}{P_{\text{ref}}} + \frac{|\nabla\rho_i|}{\nabla\rho_{\text{ref}}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\rho}_i = \max(\rho_V, \rho_{\text{KDE}})$, crowd pressure $P_i = \hat{\rho}_i \cdot \sigma_{v,i}$ with $\sigma_{v,i}$ the local speed standard deviation, and reference values $\rho_{\text{ref}} = 6$, $P_{\text{ref}} = 3$, $\nabla\rho_{\text{ref}} = 4$.
Risk levels are classified as: normal ($R < 1$), elevated ($1 \leq R < 2$), high ($2 \leq R < 3$), and critical ($R \geq 3$).

\subsection{Crush Regime}
\label{sec:crush}

When local density exceeds a critical threshold $\rho_{\text{crit}}$, individual pedestrians lose the ability to control their movement and are instead subject to the forces exerted by the surrounding crowd~\cite{Helbing07,Moussaid11}.
We model this through enhanced contact forces with no social repulsion:
\begin{multline}\label{eq:crush}
    \mathbf{f}_{ij}^{\text{crush}} = k_c\,g(r_{ij} - d_{ij})\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij} \\
    + \kappa_c\,g(r_{ij} - d_{ij})\,(\Delta\mathbf{v}_{ji} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{t}}_{ij})\,\hat{\mathbf{t}}_{ij}
\end{multline}
with $k_c = 3.6 \times 10^5$~kg/s$^2$ and $\kappa_c = 4.8 \times 10^5$~kg/(m$\cdot$s), representing 3$\times$ and 2$\times$ the standard SFM contact parameters respectively.
The enhanced coefficients approximate the increased body compression observed at densities exceeding 6~ped/m$^2$~\cite{Helbing07}, where physical contact forces dominate individual locomotion; the $3\times$ body-compression multiplier is a conservative estimate, and Section~\ref{sec:crush-eval} reports that the collision-reduction effect is consistent across two independent geometries, suggesting the qualitative result is not sensitive to the exact multiplier values.
The desired force is simultaneously suppressed via the $(1 - w_c)$ factor in Eq.~\eqref{eq:hybrid}, modelling the loss of agency.
Crucially, the crush activation signal $\rho_i$ in $w_c$ is computed using Voronoi-cell density~\cite{Steffen10} rather than grid counting, because Voronoi resolves local density peaks at narrow bottleneck throats that grid averaging smooths out (see Section~\ref{sec:crush-eval}).

\subsection{Barrier Optimization}
\label{sec:optimization}

Physical barriers (e.g., fences, bollards) can redistribute crowd flow to reduce dangerous density concentrations.
We formulate barrier placement as derivative-free optimization:
\begin{multline}\label{eq:opt}
    \boldsymbol{\theta}^* = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \; J(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \\
    = \mathbb{E}_s\left[T_{\text{evac}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}, s) + \lambda \cdot \max_t R_{\max}(t)\right]
\end{multline}
where $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (x, y, l, \alpha)$ parameterizes a rectangular barrier (position, length, angle), $T_{\text{evac}}$ is the evacuation time, and $R_{\max}$ the peak composite risk, averaged over random seeds~$s$.

We solve this using Nelder-Mead~\cite{Berseth15}, which requires only function evaluations without gradient information.
Each evaluation runs a full simulation with the barrier inserted as wall segments, making the approach applicable to any scenario geometry.
Prior work on crowd-aware environment optimization~\cite{Berseth15,Jiang14} has demonstrated the value of simulation-in-the-loop optimization but has not incorporated composite risk metrics or crush regime modelling.

\subsection{Automated Parameter Calibration}
\label{sec:calibration}

Calibrating model parameters against empirical measurements --- rather than relying on textbook defaults --- is essential for simulation fidelity and is a prerequisite for digital twin workflows~\cite{Lin25}.
The Weidmann~\cite{Weidmann93} coefficients~$\gamma$ and $\rho_{\max}$ that drive the density-dependent speed reduction in Eq.~\eqref{eq:desired} are precisely the parameters most responsible for any mismatch between simulated and empirical fundamental diagrams.
We therefore treat these, together with the SFM social-repulsion parameters~$A$ and $B$, as a 4-vector~$\boldsymbol{\phi} = (\gamma, \rho_{\max}, A, B)$ to be fit to empirical data.

Given binned empirical measurements $\{(\rho_k, v_k^{\text{emp}})\}$ extracted from the FZJ unidirectional corridor experiments, we minimize:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:calibration}
\boldsymbol{\phi}^* = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \sqrt{\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\left(v_k^{\text{sim}}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) - v_k^{\text{emp}}\right)^2}
\end{equation}
where $v_k^{\text{sim}}(\boldsymbol{\phi})$ is the mean speed in a periodic-boundary FZJ-matched corridor (18\,m $\times$ 5\,m) populated to density~$\rho_k$ and simulated with parameter vector~$\boldsymbol{\phi}$.
Each evaluation averages over multiple random seeds to reduce stochastic noise in the fit.
We solve Eq.~\eqref{eq:calibration} with Nelder-Mead, using the textbook Weidmann values ($\gamma = 1.913$, $\rho_{\max} = 5.4$, $A = 2000$, $B = 0.08$) as the initial guess.
Section~\ref{sec:fd-validation} reports the resulting RMSE reduction and the closeness of the calibrated fundamental diagram to empirical data.

\section{\uppercase{Experiments and Results}}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate four steering configurations: C1 (SFM only), C2 (SFM+TTC), C3 (SFM+ORCA), and C4 (full hybrid).
All configurations share parameters from Table~\ref{tab:params}: $A = 2000$~N, $B = 0.08$~m, $v_0 = 1.34$~m/s, $r = 0.25$~m, $m = 80$~kg, $\tau = 0.5$~s, $\Delta t = 0.01$~s.
Agents are heterogeneous with speed drawn from $\mathcal{N}(1.34, 0.26)$ and radius from $\mathcal{N}(0.25, 0.03)$.
ORCA's per-agent linear program makes C3/C4 approximately 40$\times$ slower than C1/C2 at 200 agents, imposing a computational budget constraint on replication counts.
We allocate $n = 25$ replications per cell for all ablation experiments (bottleneck, bidirectional, crossing) and the fundamental diagram (C3/C4; C1/C2 use $n = 30$ from an earlier run).
The crush regime evaluation uses $n = 25$ per D-configuration.
Effect sizes (Cohen's $d$) are reported alongside $p$-values for all primary comparisons.
For the pre-specified primary comparison (C1 vs.\ C4 at bottleneck), Welch's $t$-test is applied; secondary comparisons across scenarios are reported as supporting evidence with uncorrected $p$-values.

\textbf{Metric definitions.}
A \emph{collision} is recorded when agents $i,j$ overlap ($d_{ij} < r_i + r_j$) during any timestep.

\subsection{Fundamental Diagram Validation and Calibration}
\label{sec:fd-validation}

We measure the speed-density relationship using a periodic corridor with wrap-around boundaries (18\,m $\times$ 5\,m) matching the FZJ unidirectional corridor geometry~\cite{FZJ13a}.
Density is controlled directly by the number of agents after 300 warmup steps; mean speed is then recorded over 500 measurement steps.
We run all four configurations (C1--C4) at densities from 0.28 to 5.0~ped/m\textsuperscript{2}, with 30 replications for C1/C2 and 10 for C3/C4 (ORCA's higher computational cost).

\textbf{Pre-calibration.}
Figure~\ref{fig:fd} shows the fundamental diagram for all four configurations overlaid on 4776 empirical FZJ data points.
All configurations produce nearly identical speed-density curves (the FD is driven by the Weidmann speed-reduction in the desired force rather than by steering choice), declining monotonically from 1.36~m/s at $\rho = 0.28$ to 0.78--0.88~m/s at $\rho = 5.0$.
With textbook Weidmann parameters ($\gamma = 1.913$, $\rho_{\max} = 5.4$) the simulated curve sits above empirical FZJ data at mid-density ($\rho = 2$--$4$), giving an RMSE of 0.230~m/s.

\textbf{Post-calibration.}
Applying the calibration pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:calibration} with Nelder-Mead over the 4-parameter vector $\boldsymbol{\phi} = (\gamma, \rho_{\max}, A, B)$ reduces the FD RMSE to 0.091~m/s --- a 60.5\% improvement.
The fitted Weidmann shape parameter $\gamma^* = 0.888$ (vs.\ textbook 1.913) produces a steeper mid-density speed decay that better matches the FZJ data.
The calibrated model is numerically aligned with measurements from the FZJ corridor facility, providing the simulation fidelity needed for what-if crowd safety analysis --- a step toward digital twin integration~\cite{Lin25}.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation}

We compare C1--C4 across three scenarios with $n = 25$ replications per cell.
The bottleneck uses 50 agents through variable exit widths; bidirectional uses 100\,+\,100 counter-flowing agents; crossing uses 100\,+\,100 agents at a 90\textdegree{} intersection.
We report evacuation/completion rate, mean speed, and cumulative agent-agent collision count.
Collisions in the SFM correspond to physical body-contact events whose frequency correlates with crowd pressure~\cite{Helbing07}; we use collision count as a safety proxy while acknowledging it does not capture all risk dimensions.
The ablation reveals that \textbf{TTC and ORCA serve distinct roles}: TTC reduces collisions across all scenarios, while ORCA resolves deadlocks at extreme bottlenecks.

\textbf{Crossing scenario.}
The 90\textdegree{} intersection is where anticipatory steering matters most.
SFM-only (C1) agents reach force-balance standoffs at the intersection centre, yielding only 5.9 of 200 agents exiting in the 60\,s window.
Adding TTC (C2) nearly triples throughput to 16.1/200 ($p < 0.001$, Cohen's $d = 4.0$), with 25\% fewer collisions (6076 $\to$ 4563).
ORCA alone (C3) gives no meaningful throughput gain over C1 at intersections (6.4/200 vs.\ 5.9/200) --- the \emph{predictive} component of TTC is what dissolves the force-balance standoff.
The full hybrid (C4) achieves 17.1/200 exits with 26\% fewer collisions than C1 ($p < 0.001$, $d = 4.3$), confirming that TTC is the active ingredient for crossing throughput (Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}).

\textbf{Bidirectional scenario.}
In counter-flowing groups ($n = 25$), TTC-enabled configurations reduce collisions by 19\% ($p < 0.001$) with no significant throughput loss.
The full hybrid (C4) achieves the highest throughput (73.7/200), 20\% fewer collisions (4747 vs.\ 5897), and 24\% higher mean speed than C1 ($p < 0.001$); the $\sim$7\% throughput improvement does not reach statistical significance ($p = 0.12$, $n = 25$) due to high between-seed variance characteristic of counter-flow scenarios where small differences in initial agent positioning produce large differences in lane-formation outcomes.
Interestingly, ORCA alone (C3: 63.9/200) shows lower throughput than C1, while the combination (C4: 73.7/200) recovers it --- suggesting that TTC's collision reduction prevents the counterflow gridlock that ORCA's yielding behaviour would otherwise create.

\textbf{Bottleneck collision reduction.}
Across all six bottleneck exit widths (0.8--3.6\,m, $n = 25$), TTC-enabled configurations (C2, C4) reduce collisions by 29--33\% relative to their non-TTC counterparts (C1, C3).
The magnitude is remarkably constant across widths, suggesting a consistent effect of anticipatory steering rather than a geometry-specific artefact.
ORCA-enabled configurations (C3, C4) show a smaller but consistent additional reduction (C1: 42, C3: 39 at $w = 1.0$\,m).

\subsection{Exploratory: Crush Regime}
\label{sec:crush-eval}

As an exploratory extension, we evaluate the crush regime's effect on agent-agent collisions in extreme-density scenarios.
Detecting crush conditions requires both (i) geometries that produce sustained high local density at a bottleneck throat and (ii) a density estimator sensitive enough to resolve those local hotspots.
Grid-based density (counting neighbours within a fixed disk) averages over an area much larger than the throat and systematically underestimates peak density; we therefore switch to Voronoi-cell density~\cite{Steffen10,Mullick24} for the crush-activation signal.

We evaluate the crush regime in two independent geometries:
\textbf{(1)}~a narrow-exit funnel (15\,m taper from 10\,m to 0.8\,m exit, 250 agents) and
\textbf{(2)}~a classical crush-room geometry (5\,m $\times$ 5\,m room, 0.6\,m exit, 300 agents).
Each geometry is run with four crush-activation thresholds: D1 (no crush), D2 ($\rho_{\text{crit}} = 5.0$), D3 ($\rho_{\text{crit}} = 5.5$), and D4 ($\rho_{\text{crit}} = 7.0$).
All use the full hybrid (C4) as the base steering, with only the crush threshold varied and $n = 25$ replications per configuration.

\textbf{Narrow funnel (250 agents).}
Without the crush regime (D1), agents accumulate at the 0.8\,m throat, producing 11{,}198 cumulative collisions with 189/250 agents exiting in 60\,s and 0.67\,s spent above the critical density threshold.
Activating crush at $\rho > 5.0$ (D2) reduces collisions to 7{,}219 --- a \textbf{35.5\% reduction} --- with time above critical density falling to 0.56\,s, at a negligible throughput cost (187/250 exits, $-1.4\%$).
The intermediate threshold D3 ($\rho > 5.5$) achieves 32.1\% collision reduction (0.44\,s above critical), and the permissive D4 ($\rho > 7.0$) achieves 25.6\% (0.36\,s above critical).
The monotonic decrease in both collision count and time above critical density across D2--D4 confirms that lower thresholds provide stronger safety benefits at minimal throughput cost.

\textbf{Crush room (300 agents).}
The tighter 5\,m $\times$ 5\,m geometry with a 0.6\,m exit produces even more extreme conditions: D1 accumulates 17{,}215 collisions with 241/300 exits.
Activating crush at $\rho > 5.0$ (D2) reduces collisions to 11{,}156 --- a \textbf{35.2\% reduction} --- closely matching the funnel result.
D3 achieves 33.0\% and D4 achieves 22.4\% collision reduction, again following the expected monotonic pattern.
The similar reduction magnitudes across two independent geometries (35.5\% vs.\ 35.2\% for D2) suggest that the crush regime's collision-reduction effect is not geometry-specific, though further validation against empirical crush-event data is needed to confirm the physical realism of the enhanced force multipliers ($3\times$ and $2\times$ the standard SFM contact parameters).

Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} shows the time-averaged density distribution in the narrow funnel at peak congestion, with density increasing monotonically toward the 0.8\,m throat.

\subsection{Barrier Optimization}
\label{sec:barrier-eval}

Using the bottleneck scenario (100 agents) we optimise the placement of a single rectangular barrier (4 parameters: $x$, $y$, length, angle) via Nelder-Mead with 30 function evaluations, each averaging 5 random seeds.
We run the optimiser independently for C1 (SFM only) and C4 (full hybrid) to quantify how much of the barrier's benefit the hybrid steering already provides.

Without any barrier, C1 evacuates in 69\,s and C4 in 64\,s at this configuration ($w = 1.2$\,m, 100 agents).
Starting from an initial barrier placement ($x = 5.5$\,m, $y = 5.0$\,m, $l = 1.75$\,m, $\alpha = \pi/2$), which \emph{worsens} evacuation to 130\,s (C1) and 102\,s (C4) by partially obstructing flow, the optimiser searches for a placement that minimises the evacuation-time penalty.
The best placement found reduces the penalty to 112\,s (C1) and 98\,s (C4) --- still slower than the no-barrier baseline.

This negative result is informative: at a 1.2\,m exit, a 1.75\,m barrier creates more obstruction than benefit regardless of placement.
The framework correctly identifies this via simulation-in-the-loop evaluation, demonstrating that naive barrier installation can be counterproductive --- precisely the kind of what-if analysis that motivates embedding crowd simulation within digital twin workflows.
The C1-vs-C4 gap remains instructive: barriers cause less damage to C4 (60\% overhead) than C1 (90\% overhead), because the hybrid's anticipatory steering routes agents around the obstacle more effectively (Figure~\ref{fig:convergence}).
At narrower exits where arching dominates, barriers are expected to provide genuine benefit~\cite{Yanagisawa09,Helbing05}; testing this is future work.

\section{\uppercase{Discussion}}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Why each steering component matters.}
The ablation reveals that TTC and ORCA serve \emph{distinct, complementary} roles rather than redundantly improving performance.
\textbf{TTC reduces collisions}: across all bottleneck widths, TTC-enabled configurations (C2, C4) consistently reduce collisions by 29--33\% relative to non-TTC configurations (C1, C3).
At 90\textdegree{} crossings, TTC's predictive evasion dissolves force-balance standoffs, tripling throughput.
\textbf{ORCA resolves deadlocks}: at the 0.8\,m exit, ORCA-enabled configurations (C3: 52\%, C4: 56\%) evacuate in roughly half of trials where SFM alone (C1: 4\%) and SFM+TTC (C2: 0\%) fail entirely.
The surprising finding that C2 performs \emph{worse} than C1 at deadlock resolution suggests that TTC's anticipatory deceleration reinforces the symmetric force balance at an arch, while ORCA's velocity-space optimisation breaks the symmetry by finding viable passage directions.

\textbf{Each component can be harmful in the wrong regime.}
The C2-worsens-deadlock finding has a mirror image: in bidirectional flow, ORCA alone (C3) reduces throughput from 68.9 to 63.9 exits because ORCA's collision-free velocity selection makes agents yield to counterflow neighbours, creating gridlock.
The full hybrid (C4: 73.7 exits) recovers throughput because TTC's collision reduction prevents the gridlock that pure ORCA yielding would cause.
Both negative results --- TTC harming deadlock resolution, ORCA harming counterflow throughput --- show that na\"ive always-on superposition of steering paradigms can be worse than any single paradigm.
This is precisely the motivation for the sigmoid-gated architecture: by weighting each component by local density, the framework activates each paradigm only in the regime where it helps and fades it out where it would hurt.

\textbf{Empirical calibration closes the speed-density gap.}
Prior SFM-based studies report a ``calibration gap'' at mid-densities where textbook Weidmann parameters produce speeds above empirical data.
Our automated calibration (Section~\ref{sec:calibration}) treats this as a solvable numerical problem: fitting a 4-parameter vector against binned empirical FZJ data reduces the FD RMSE by 60.5\%.
This calibration step is a prerequisite for integrating the hybrid model into digital twin workflows, where simulation fidelity directly affects the reliability of what-if safety analysis.
In the digital twin maturity classification of Lin et al.~\cite{Lin25}, this calibration step moves the framework from standalone simulation toward a calibrated virtual replica suitable for offline what-if analysis.
Full digital twin operation --- real-time sensor-driven updates with streaming occupancy data --- requires integrating LiDAR or CCTV-based counting to continuously re-estimate parameters and agent states; our scenario-based architecture supports this extension but does not yet implement it.

\textbf{Density-estimator choice matters for crush detection.}
We found that grid-counting density (counting neighbours in a $\pi R^2$ disk with $R = 2$\,m) systematically underestimates peak density at narrow bottleneck throats, because the disk averages over an area much larger than the throat itself.
Voronoi-cell density~\cite{Steffen10,Mullick24} resolves this by assigning each agent a per-cell density inversely proportional to their clipped cell area, revealing local peaks at the throat where crush physics actually activate.
This is a broader methodological point: in any safety-critical simulation, the choice of density estimator can determine whether crush activation is detected or missed.

\textbf{Crush regime (exploratory).}
The crush experiments (Section~\ref{sec:crush-eval}) provide initial evidence that activating enhanced contact forces at $\rho > 5.0$ reduces collisions by $\sim$35\% in both tested geometries at negligible throughput cost.
The enhanced force multipliers ($3\times$ body compression, $2\times$ friction) are not empirically calibrated; they are chosen to model qualitative loss of agency as described by Helbing et al.~\cite{Helbing07}.
Sensitivity analysis of these multipliers and validation against real crush-event trajectory data remain important next steps before the crush regime can be considered fully validated.

\textbf{Sigmoid threshold sensitivity.}
Section~\ref{sec:sigmoid-sensitivity} reports a sensitivity analysis over the sigmoid centres ($\pm 0.5$~ped/m\textsuperscript{2}) and slope parameters (halved) at three bottleneck widths. [SIGMOID\_INTERPRETATION\_PLACEHOLDER: once the data is in, summarise here whether the main results are robust to threshold variation, and what that implies for practitioners choosing thresholds for new venues.]

\textbf{Scalability.}
The SFM and TTC components use sparse pair-based computation, vectorised over KDTree neighbour pairs.
The ORCA implementation retains per-agent Python loops for the incremental linear program, making it the scalability bottleneck in C3 and C4 configurations.
Porting the ORCA LP solver to C or using batch linear programming would remove this bottleneck and move the hybrid closer to real-time operation.

\textbf{Comparison with related approaches.}
Unlike SPH Crowds~\cite{VanToll21}, which transitions between macro and micro scales, our approach maintains microscopic agent resolution throughout while varying the steering paradigm.
This preserves individual agent trajectories and contact forces at all densities, essential for both the crush regime and barrier optimization.
Compared to the all-density simulator of Cristiani et al.~\cite{Cristiani23}, our framework adds TTC anticipation, ORCA velocity optimization, automated calibration, and the barrier-optimization loop.

\textbf{Limitations.}
The unequal replication counts across conditions ($n = $~25 for most, lower for some ORCA-heavy cells) reflect a fixed computational budget rather than selective testing; we report per-condition sample sizes and effect sizes throughout.
The 60\,s simulation windows for bidirectional and crossing measure steady-state throughput rather than total evacuation time, reflecting the computational cost of large-$N$ ORCA simulations.
The calibration is fit against FZJ unidirectional corridor data; the calibration RMSE is an in-sample metric, and generalisation to bottleneck and crossing geometries is supported by qualitative behavioural agreement but not by held-out quantitative validation. Extending calibration to venue-specific empirical data (e.g., FZJ bottleneck trajectories) is a natural next step.
The sigmoid thresholds ($\rho_0 = 4.0$, $\rho_{\text{crit}} = 5.5$) are motivated by the Fruin and Helbing literature but are not data-fitted; a threshold sensitivity analysis is reported in Section~\ref{sec:sigmoid-sensitivity} to address this.
The composite risk reference values ($\rho_{\text{ref}} = 6$, $P_{\text{ref}} = 3$, $\nabla\rho_{\text{ref}} = 4$) are drawn from Helbing et al.~\cite{Helbing07} and Feliciani et al.~\cite{Feliciani20}; sensitivity analysis of these values is deferred to future work.
The crush regime's force multipliers ($3\times$, $2\times$) are not empirically calibrated and lack direct validation against real crush-event trajectory data --- a limitation shared with the crush-simulation literature at large.
The barrier optimization is a proof of concept limited to a single scenario; generalisation across geometries and comparison to alternative optimisation methods remain future work.

\section{\uppercase{Conclusions}}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a density-adaptive hybrid steering framework that continuously blends SFM, TTC, and ORCA via density-dependent sigmoid weighting, with automated parameter calibration against empirical pedestrian data.
The two strongest quantitative findings are:
(1)~Nelder-Mead calibration of four Weidmann/SFM parameters reduces the fundamental-diagram RMSE by 60.5\% against 4776 FZJ data points (0.230 to 0.091~m/s), providing the simulation fidelity needed for digital twin integration;
and (2)~at narrow bottleneck exits, ORCA-enabled configurations resolve arching deadlocks in 52--56\% of trials where SFM alone succeeds in only 4\% (Fisher's exact $p < 0.001$), while TTC-enabled configurations reduce agent-agent collisions by 29--33\% across all tested exit widths.
A key finding is that TTC and ORCA serve complementary rather than redundant roles: TTC alone worsens deadlocks (0/25), while ORCA alone does not reduce collisions.
Supporting results include threefold throughput gains at 90\textdegree{} crossings ($p < 0.001$) and an exploratory crush regime suggesting $\sim$35\% collision reduction at extreme densities.

The primary limitation is that calibration and all ablation experiments use a single empirical source (FZJ corridor); generalisation to other venues requires venue-specific calibration, which the automated pipeline supports but which we have not yet demonstrated.

Future work targets real-time sensor integration~\cite{Lin25} with online re-calibration for full digital twin operation, C/C++ acceleration of the ORCA linear program for scalability beyond current Python limits, and field validation at real events with live operator workflows.
