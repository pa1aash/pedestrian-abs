\keywords{Crowd Simulation, Digital Twin, Social Force Model, Collision Avoidance, Crowd Safety, Agent-Based Modeling.}

\abstract{Crowd disasters such as the 2022 Itaewon crush (159 deaths) highlight the need for predictive crowd safety tools. We present CrowdTwin, a hybrid agent-based digital twin framework that continuously blends Social Force Model, time-to-collision, and ORCA steering using density-dependent sigmoid weights, coupled with multi-method density estimation, a composite risk metric, derivative-free barrier optimization, and automated parameter calibration against empirical data. Validation against FZJ empirical data (952 pedestrians, 4776 fundamental diagram points) establishes a calibrated virtual replica (Digital Twin Level~2) with \textbf{60.5\% RMSE reduction} (0.23 to 0.09~m/s) via Nelder-Mead fitting of the Weidmann speed-density parameters. Ablation experiments across four scenarios ($n = 20$ replications, Welch's $t$-test) show complementary benefits: at narrow bottleneck exits the hybrid achieves 7--9\% faster evacuation ($p < 0.01$) and 32\% fewer agent-agent collisions; at 90\textdegree{} crossings, TTC-enabled configurations triple throughput ($p = 0.001$); at 0.8\,m exits the hybrid resolves arching deadlocks in 60\% of trials where SFM alone succeeds in 5\%. A Voronoi-density-driven crush regime caps dangerous compression at sustained densities above 5.5~ped/m\textsuperscript{2}.}

\section{\uppercase{Introduction}}
\label{sec:introduction}

Crowd disasters remain a persistent threat to public safety worldwide.
The 2022 Itaewon crowd crush in Seoul killed 159 people in a narrow alley during Halloween celebrations~\cite{Lee24}, while the 2010 Love Parade disaster in Duisburg resulted in 21 deaths at a tunnel bottleneck~\cite{Helbing12}.
These tragedies share a common pattern: localised density exceeds survivable thresholds before organizers detect the hazard.
Predictive tools that can anticipate dangerous density buildups and evaluate mitigation strategies \emph{before} an event are therefore critical for crowd safety management.

Pedestrian crowd simulation has matured considerably since the seminal Social Force Model (SFM) of Helbing and Moln\'{a}r~\cite{Helbing95}.
Force-based approaches~\cite{Helbing00}, velocity-obstacle methods~\cite{Fiorini98,VandenBerg11}, and anticipatory models based on time-to-collision (TTC)~\cite{Karamouzas14} each capture different aspects of pedestrian behaviour.
However, no single paradigm performs well across the full density spectrum: the SFM excels at contact-rich high-density regimes but produces unrealistic paths at low density, while ORCA~\cite{VandenBerg11} yields smooth collision-free trajectories in sparse crowds but cannot model the physical compression forces that dominate crush scenarios.
Existing simulation tools typically commit to a single model, sacrificing accuracy at density regimes where that model is weakest.

The Digital Twin (DT) concept~\cite{Grieves17} offers a unifying architecture: a virtual replica of a physical venue, continuously updated with sensor data and capable of what-if analysis.
Recent work has explored crowd digital twins for railway stations~\cite{Gatta24} and evacuation planning~\cite{Lin25}, yet no published system integrates hybrid multi-paradigm steering, multi-method density estimation, composite risk metrics, and derivative-free barrier optimization within a single DT loop.

This paper presents \textsc{CrowdTwin}, a hybrid agent-based digital twin framework for crowd safety assessment.
Our contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A \textbf{density-weighted hybrid steering model} that continuously blends SFM, TTC, and ORCA forces using sigmoid activation functions of local density, yielding appropriate behaviour from free flow through crush conditions.
    \item \textbf{Multi-method density estimation} combining grid counting, Voronoi tessellation, and kernel density estimation, feeding a composite risk metric that integrates density, crowd pressure, and density gradients.
    \item \textbf{Automated parameter calibration} via derivative-free optimization of Weidmann speed-density parameters against empirical FZJ data, achieving Digital Twin Level~2 by closing the speed-density calibration gap between simulation and real pedestrian measurements.
    \item \textbf{Derivative-free barrier optimization} using CMA-ES to place physical barriers that minimize composite risk, demonstrated on bottleneck evacuation scenarios.
    \item A \textbf{density-dependent crush regime} with enhanced contact forces, activated by local Voronoi density estimation, that models the loss of individual agency observed in real crowd crushes.
    \item \textbf{Integration within a Digital Twin architecture} supporting continuous agent injection, periodic boundary calibration against empirical data, and scenario-based what-if analysis across five standard pedestrian scenarios.
\end{enumerate}

We validate the framework against the Weidmann fundamental diagram~\cite{Seyfried05} and 4776 empirical data points from the FZJ Pedestrian Dynamics Data Archive~\cite{FZJ13a}, demonstrating that automated parameter calibration reduces the speed-density RMSE by 60.5\%, establishing a Level~2 Digital Twin.
Ablation experiments ($n = 20$ replications, Welch's $t$-test) across bottleneck, bidirectional, and crossing scenarios quantify the complementary contribution of each steering component, while crush experiments in narrow-exit funnel and room geometries show that the Voronoi-driven crush regime caps dangerous density levels.

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows.
Section~2 reviews related work in pedestrian simulation, crowd safety metrics, and digital twins.
Section~3 presents the framework architecture including the hybrid steering model, density estimation, and optimization components.
Section~4 describes the experimental setup and results.
Section~5 discusses findings and limitations.
Section~6 concludes with future directions.

\section{\uppercase{Related Work}}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Force-Based and Velocity-Based Models}

The Social Force Model (SFM) introduced by Helbing and Moln\'{a}r~\cite{Helbing95} represents pedestrians as particles subject to desired, social repulsive, and contact forces.
The extended model~\cite{Helbing00} added body compression and sliding friction forces that activate upon physical contact, enabling simulation of dense crowds and panic scenarios.
Reynolds~\cite{Reynolds99} proposed steering behaviours for autonomous agents, while Fiorini and Shiller~\cite{Fiorini98} formalized velocity obstacles (VO) for collision avoidance.
Van den Berg et al.~\cite{VandenBerg11} introduced Optimal Reciprocal Collision Avoidance (ORCA), which constructs half-plane velocity constraints and solves a linear program to find the optimal collision-free velocity.
Karamouzas et al.~\cite{Karamouzas14} demonstrated that pedestrians respond to anticipated time-to-collision with an inverse power-law force, providing an anticipatory steering mechanism.
Guy et al.~\cite{Guy10} developed PLEdestrians, optimizing agent parameters to match real pedestrian trajectories.

These models are typically deployed individually. Force-based approaches handle contact-rich scenarios well but produce oscillatory paths at low density; velocity-based methods yield smooth trajectories but cannot represent physical compression. No published system combines all three paradigms in a density-adaptive framework.

\subsection{Hybrid and Multi-Regime Models}

Several authors have explored combining simulation paradigms.
Van Toll et al.~\cite{VanToll21} proposed SPH-based crowd simulation that transitions between microscopic and macroscopic scales.
Moussa\"{\i}d et al.~\cite{Moussaid11} developed heuristic-based models calibrated to empirical data from bottleneck experiments, though these remain single-paradigm.
Cristiani et al.~\cite{Cristiani23} introduced a multi-scale simulator handling all density regimes through model switching.
Gaudou et al.~\cite{Gaudou24} surveyed agent-based crowd simulation approaches, identifying the need for models that adapt their resolution to local conditions.

Existing hybrids typically combine at most two paradigms (e.g., macro-micro coupling) and use hard switching rather than continuous blending. None combine SFM contact forces, TTC anticipation, and ORCA velocity optimization in a single framework with density-dependent weighting.

\subsection{Crowd Safety Metrics and Density Estimation}

Fruin~\cite{Fruin71} established the Level of Service framework relating pedestrian density to comfort and safety.
Helbing et al.~\cite{Helbing07} defined crowd pressure as the product of density and velocity variance, providing a metric sensitive to the turbulent conditions preceding crowd crushes.
Steffen and Seyfried~\cite{Steffen10} demonstrated that Voronoi tessellation provides more accurate local density estimates than grid-based counting in heterogeneous crowds.
Tordeux et al.~\cite{Tordeux15} compared density estimation methods and found that the choice of method significantly affects measured fundamental diagrams.
Mullick et al.~\cite{Mullick24} extended Voronoi density to handle boundary effects through mirror-point techniques.
Feliciani et al.~\cite{Feliciani20} proposed congestion and danger metrics based on density gradients, arguing that spatial variation in density is a stronger predictor of incidents than density alone.

No existing framework combines multiple density estimators (grid, Voronoi, KDE) into a composite risk metric that integrates density magnitude, crowd pressure, and density gradients.

\subsection{Digital Twins for Crowd Management}

Grieves and Vickers~\cite{Grieves17} formalized the Digital Twin concept as a virtual replica connected to its physical counterpart through data exchange.
Croatti and Ricci~\cite{Croatti26} proposed an agent-based architecture for crowd digital twins (CDT) supporting real-time monitoring and what-if analysis.
Gatta et al.~\cite{Gatta24} developed a railway station CDT integrating pedestrian simulation with IoT sensor data.
Lin et al.~\cite{Lin25} surveyed digital twin applications for emergency evacuation, identifying crowd simulation fidelity as the primary bottleneck.

Published CDT systems typically embed a single simulation model and do not include optimization components for infrastructure design. Our framework addresses this gap by integrating hybrid steering, composite risk assessment, and barrier optimization within the DT loop.

\section{\uppercase{Framework Architecture}}
\label{sec:framework}

The CrowdTwin framework consists of four integrated components: (1)~a hybrid steering model that blends force-based and velocity-based paradigms, (2)~multi-method density estimation with composite risk assessment, (3)~a density-dependent crush regime, and (4)~derivative-free barrier optimization. We describe each in turn.

\subsection{Hybrid Steering Model}
\label{sec:hybrid}

Each agent~$i$ is characterized by position~$\mathbf{x}_i$, velocity~$\mathbf{v}_i$, goal~$\mathbf{g}_i$, radius~$r_i$, desired speed~$v_i^0$, mass~$m_i$, and relaxation time~$\tau_i$.
The total force on agent~$i$ is:
\begin{multline}\label{eq:hybrid}
    \mathbf{F}_i = (1 - w_c)\,\mathbf{F}_i^{\text{des}} + \mathbf{F}_i^{\text{SFM}} + \mathbf{F}_i^{\text{TTC}} \\
    + w_o\,\mathbf{F}_i^{\text{ORCA}} + w_c\,\mathbf{F}_i^{\text{crush}} + \mathbf{F}_i^{\text{wall}}
\end{multline}
where $w_o(\rho_i)$ and $w_c(\rho_i)$ are density-dependent weights controlling the contribution of ORCA and crush forces respectively.

\textbf{Desired Force.}
The goal-seeking force drives agents toward their destination with a density-dependent speed reduction following the Weidmann fundamental diagram~\cite{Seyfried05}:
\begin{gather}\label{eq:desired}
    \mathbf{F}_i^{\text{des}} = \frac{m_i}{\tau_i}\left(v_i^{\text{eff}}(\rho_i)\,\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i - \mathbf{v}_i\right) \\
    v_i^{\text{eff}} = v_i^0\left(1 - e^{-\gamma(1/\rho_i - 1/\rho_{\max})}\right) \notag
\end{gather}
with $\gamma = 1.913$ and $\rho_{\max} = 5.4$~ped/m$^2$.

\textbf{Social Force Model.}
Agent-agent interaction follows Helbing et al.~\cite{Helbing00} with social repulsion, body compression, and sliding friction:
\begin{multline}\label{eq:sfm}
    \mathbf{f}_{ij} = A\,e^{(r_{ij} - d_{ij})/B}\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij} + k\,g(r_{ij} - d_{ij})\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij} \\
    + \kappa\,g(r_{ij} - d_{ij})\,(\Delta\mathbf{v}_{ji} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{t}}_{ij})\,\hat{\mathbf{t}}_{ij}
\end{multline}
where $r_{ij} = r_i + r_j$ is the sum of radii, $d_{ij} = \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\|$ the distance, $g(x) = \max(0, x)$, and parameters $A = 2000$~N, $B = 0.08$~m, $k = 1.2 \times 10^5$~kg/s$^2$, $\kappa = 2.4 \times 10^5$~kg/(m$\cdot$s).

\textbf{Time-to-Collision Force.}
Following Karamouzas et al.~\cite{Karamouzas14}, agents anticipate collisions and apply repulsive forces that decay exponentially with time-to-collision~$\tau_{ij}$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ttc}
    F_{\text{TTC}} = \frac{k_{\text{ttc}}\,e^{-\tau_{ij}/\tau_0}}{\tau_{ij}^2}, \quad
    \tau_{ij} = \frac{-b - \sqrt{b^2 - ac}}{a}
\end{equation}
with $k_{\text{ttc}} = 1.5$, $\tau_0 = 3.0$~s, and the quadratic coefficients derived from relative position and velocity.

\textbf{ORCA.}
At low density, ORCA~\cite{VandenBerg11} constructs half-plane velocity constraints from each neighbour and solves a 2D linear program to find the velocity closest to the preferred velocity while satisfying all constraints. The resulting velocity is converted to a force: $\mathbf{F}_i^{\text{ORCA}} = m_i(\mathbf{v}_i^{\text{ORCA}} - \mathbf{v}_i) / \tau_{\text{ORCA}}$.

\textbf{Density-Dependent Weighting.}
The ORCA and crush contributions are modulated by sigmoid functions of local density:
\begin{gather}\label{eq:sigmoid}
    w_o(\rho) = 1 - \sigma(\rho;\, 4.0,\, 2.0), \;\;
    w_c(\rho) = \sigma(\rho;\, 5.5,\, 3.0) \\
    \sigma(x; x_0, k) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-k(x - x_0)}} \notag
\end{gather}
At low density ($\rho < 2$), ORCA dominates ($w_o \approx 1$); at high density ($\rho > 6$), crush forces take over ($w_c \approx 1$).
The SFM contributes at all densities, providing the contact mechanics that prevent agent overlap.

\subsection{Density Estimation and Composite Risk}
\label{sec:density}

We estimate local density using three complementary methods.
\textbf{Grid counting} computes $\rho_G(i) = |\{j : \|\mathbf{x}_j - \mathbf{x}_i\| < R\}| / (\pi R^2)$ with $R = 2.0$~m.
\textbf{Voronoi tessellation}~\cite{Steffen10} assigns $\rho_V(i) = 1 / A_i^{\text{Vor}}$ where $A_i^{\text{Vor}}$ is the clipped Voronoi cell area, using mirror points for boundary handling~\cite{Mullick24}.
\textbf{Kernel density estimation} uses a Gaussian kernel with bandwidth $h = 1.0$.

The composite risk metric~\cite{Feliciani20,Helbing07} combines density, crowd pressure, and density gradient:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:risk}
    R_i = \frac{\hat{\rho}_i}{\rho_{\text{ref}}} \left(1 + \frac{P_i}{P_{\text{ref}}} + \frac{|\nabla\rho_i|}{\nabla\rho_{\text{ref}}}\right)
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\rho}_i = \max(\rho_V, \rho_{\text{KDE}})$, crowd pressure $P_i = \hat{\rho}_i \cdot \sigma_{v,i}$ with $\sigma_{v,i}$ the local speed standard deviation, and reference values $\rho_{\text{ref}} = 6$, $P_{\text{ref}} = 3$, $\nabla\rho_{\text{ref}} = 4$.
Risk levels are classified as: normal ($R < 1$), elevated ($1 \leq R < 2$), high ($2 \leq R < 3$), and critical ($R \geq 3$).

\subsection{Crush Regime}
\label{sec:crush}

When local density exceeds a critical threshold $\rho_{\text{crit}}$, individual pedestrians lose the ability to control their movement and are instead subject to the forces exerted by the surrounding crowd~\cite{Helbing07,Moussaid11}.
We model this through enhanced contact forces with no social repulsion:
\begin{multline}\label{eq:crush}
    \mathbf{f}_{ij}^{\text{crush}} = k_c\,g(r_{ij} - d_{ij})\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{ij} \\
    + \kappa_c\,g(r_{ij} - d_{ij})\,(\Delta\mathbf{v}_{ji} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{t}}_{ij})\,\hat{\mathbf{t}}_{ij}
\end{multline}
with $k_c = 3.6 \times 10^5$~kg/s$^2$ and $\kappa_c = 4.8 \times 10^5$~kg/(m$\cdot$s), representing 3$\times$ and 2$\times$ the standard SFM contact parameters respectively.
The desired force is simultaneously suppressed via the $(1 - w_c)$ factor in Eq.~\eqref{eq:hybrid}, modelling the loss of agency.
Crucially, the crush activation signal $\rho_i$ in $w_c$ is computed using Voronoi-cell density~\cite{Steffen10} rather than grid counting, because Voronoi resolves local density peaks at narrow bottleneck throats that grid averaging smooths out (see Section~\ref{sec:crush-eval}).

\subsection{Barrier Optimization}
\label{sec:optimization}

Physical barriers (e.g., fences, bollards) can redistribute crowd flow to reduce dangerous density concentrations.
We formulate barrier placement as derivative-free optimization:
\begin{multline}\label{eq:opt}
    \boldsymbol{\theta}^* = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \; J(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \\
    = \mathbb{E}_s\left[T_{\text{evac}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}, s) + \lambda \cdot \max_t R_{\max}(t)\right]
\end{multline}
where $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (x, y, l, \alpha)$ parameterizes a rectangular barrier (position, length, angle), $T_{\text{evac}}$ is the evacuation time, and $R_{\max}$ the peak composite risk, averaged over random seeds~$s$.

We solve this using Nelder-Mead or CMA-ES~\cite{Berseth15}, both of which require only function evaluations without gradient information.
Each evaluation runs a full simulation with the barrier inserted as wall segments, making the approach applicable to any scenario geometry.
Prior work on crowd-aware environment optimization~\cite{Berseth15,Jiang14} has demonstrated the value of simulation-in-the-loop optimization but has not incorporated composite risk metrics or crush regime modelling.

\subsection{Parameter Calibration for Digital Twin Level~2}
\label{sec:calibration}

A defining property of a Level~2 Digital Twin in the Lin et al.~\cite{Lin25} hierarchy is that model parameters are tuned against measurements from a specific physical counterpart, rather than taken from textbook defaults.
The Weidmann~\cite{Seyfried05} coefficients~$\gamma$ and $\rho_{\max}$ that drive the density-dependent speed reduction in Eq.~\eqref{eq:desired} are precisely the parameters most responsible for any mismatch between simulated and empirical fundamental diagrams.
We therefore treat these, together with the SFM social-repulsion parameters~$A$ and $B$, as a 4-vector~$\boldsymbol{\phi} = (\gamma, \rho_{\max}, A, B)$ to be fit to empirical data.

Given binned empirical measurements $\{(\rho_k, v_k^{\text{emp}})\}$ extracted from the FZJ unidirectional corridor experiments, we minimize:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:calibration}
\boldsymbol{\phi}^* = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\phi}} \sqrt{\frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\left(v_k^{\text{sim}}(\boldsymbol{\phi}) - v_k^{\text{emp}}\right)^2}
\end{equation}
where $v_k^{\text{sim}}(\boldsymbol{\phi})$ is the mean speed in a periodic-boundary FZJ-matched corridor (18\,m $\times$ 5\,m) populated to density~$\rho_k$ and simulated with parameter vector~$\boldsymbol{\phi}$.
Each evaluation averages over multiple random seeds to reduce stochastic noise in the fit.
We solve Eq.~\eqref{eq:calibration} with Nelder-Mead, using the textbook Weidmann values ($\gamma = 1.913$, $\rho_{\max} = 5.4$, $A = 2000$, $B = 0.08$) as the initial guess.
Section~\ref{sec:fd-validation} reports the resulting RMSE reduction and the closeness of the calibrated fundamental diagram to empirical data.

\section{\uppercase{Experiments and Results}}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate four steering configurations: C1 (SFM only), C2 (SFM+TTC), C3 (SFM+ORCA), and C4 (full hybrid).
All configurations share parameters from Table~\ref{tab:params}: $A = 2000$~N, $B = 0.08$~m, $v_0 = 1.34$~m/s, $r = 0.25$~m, $m = 80$~kg, $\tau = 0.5$~s, $\Delta t = 0.01$~s.
Agents are heterogeneous with speed drawn from $\mathcal{N}(1.34, 0.26)$ and radius from $\mathcal{N}(0.25, 0.03)$.
The fundamental diagram uses 30 replications for C1/C2 and 10 for C3/C4 (where ORCA's per-agent linear program incurs higher computational cost).
The head-to-head bottleneck comparison (C1 vs.\ C4) uses $n = 20$ replications to enable significance testing via Welch's $t$-test; bidirectional C1 uses $n = 20$ for the same reason while C4 remains at $n = 5$ due to ORCA cost.
Crossing and other ablation cells use 5 replications.
We report means and 95\% confidence intervals throughout.

\subsection{Fundamental Diagram Validation and Calibration}
\label{sec:fd-validation}

We measure the speed-density relationship using a periodic corridor with wrap-around boundaries (18\,m $\times$ 5\,m) matching the FZJ unidirectional corridor geometry~\cite{FZJ13a}.
Density is controlled directly by the number of agents after 300 warmup steps; mean speed is then recorded over 500 measurement steps.
We run all four configurations (C1--C4) at densities from 0.28 to 5.0~ped/m\textsuperscript{2}, with 30 replications for C1/C2 and 10 for C3/C4 (ORCA's higher computational cost).

\textbf{Pre-calibration.}
Figure~\ref{fig:fd} shows the fundamental diagram for all four configurations overlaid on 4776 empirical FZJ data points.
All configurations produce nearly identical speed-density curves (the FD is driven by the Weidmann speed-reduction in the desired force rather than by steering choice), declining monotonically from 1.36~m/s at $\rho = 0.28$ to 0.78--0.88~m/s at $\rho = 5.0$.
With textbook Weidmann parameters ($\gamma = 1.913$, $\rho_{\max} = 5.4$) the simulated curve sits above empirical FZJ data at mid-density ($\rho = 2$--$4$), giving an RMSE of 0.230~m/s.

\textbf{Post-calibration.}
Applying the calibration pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:calibration} with Nelder-Mead over the 4-parameter vector $\boldsymbol{\phi} = (\gamma, \rho_{\max}, A, B)$ reduces the FD RMSE to 0.091~m/s --- a 60.5\% improvement.
The fitted Weidmann shape parameter $\gamma^* = 0.888$ (vs.\ textbook 1.913) produces a steeper mid-density speed decay that better matches the FZJ data.
This calibrated virtual replica constitutes the Level~2 Digital Twin: a simulation model numerically aligned with measurements from a specific physical counterpart (the FZJ corridor facility), suitable for what-if analysis of that geometry.

\subsection{Ablation Study}
\label{sec:ablation}

We compare C1--C4 across three scenarios using 5 base replications per cell, extended to 20 for the head-to-head C1-vs-C4 bottleneck comparison to enable significance testing (see Section~\ref{sec:bottleneck-evac}).
The bottleneck runs at 50 agents through variable exit widths; bidirectional uses 100\,+\,100 counter-flowing agents in a 20\,m corridor; crossing uses 100\,+\,100 agents at a 90\textdegree{} intersection.
We report three complementary metrics: evacuation/completion rate, mean agent speed, and cumulative agent-agent collision count (a direct safety proxy).

\textbf{Crossing scenario.}
The 90\textdegree{} intersection is the scenario where anticipatory steering matters most.
SFM-only (C1) agents reach force-balance standoffs at the intersection centre, yielding only 6 of 200 agents exiting in the 60\,s window.
Adding TTC (C2) nearly triples throughput to 16/200, and the full hybrid (C4) achieves 18/200 with 22\% fewer collisions than C1 ($p < 0.001$; Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}).
ORCA alone (C3) gives no meaningful gain over C1 at intersections --- the \emph{predictive} component of TTC is what dissolves the force-balance deadlock.

\textbf{Bidirectional scenario.}
In counter-flowing groups, C4 achieves a statistically significant 19\% collision reduction ($p < 0.001$) and 25\% higher individual mean speed ($p = 0.004$) compared to C1, with no statistically significant throughput loss ($p = 0.40$; C1: 70.1/200, C4: 65.4/200 exits).
The hybrid's anticipatory steering prevents agents from becoming locked in counterflow standoffs, resulting in fewer contacts and smoother individual trajectories despite comparable overall throughput.

\textbf{Bottleneck (head-to-head C1 vs C4).}
In unidirectional bottleneck flow, C4 matches or slightly exceeds C1 throughput while reducing collisions by approximately 32\% across every tested exit width (0.8--3.6\,m).
The collision-reduction magnitude is remarkably constant across widths, indicating a robust geometric effect rather than a noise artefact.

\subsection{Bottleneck Evacuation and Deadlock Resolution}
\label{sec:bottleneck-evac}

Table~\ref{tab:bottleneck} reports mean evacuation times (with 95\% confidence intervals from $n = 20$ replications) across six exit widths, with the head-to-head C1 vs.\ C4 comparison tested using Welch's $t$-test.

\textbf{Normal exits (1.0--3.6\,m).}
At widths where evacuations complete normally, the hybrid produces a statistically significant evacuation-time reduction at narrow exits: 9.3\% at $w = 1.0$\,m ($p = 0.002$) and 7.4\% at $w = 1.2$\,m ($p < 0.001$).
At $w = 1.8$\,m, a 2.9\% reduction is marginally significant ($p = 0.045$).
At wide exits ($w \geq 2.4$\,m) the configurations converge to essentially identical evacuation times ($p > 0.1$) --- the expected asymptotic behaviour as congestion disappears (Figure~\ref{fig:evac_width}).

\textbf{Narrow exit deadlock ($w = 0.8$\,m).}
At a 0.8\,m exit with 100 agents, SFM alone produces a classical ``arching'' deadlock~\cite{Helbing00} at the exit throat: in 19 of 20 replications, C1 fails to evacuate all agents within a 600\,s window.
The full hybrid (C4) breaks the deadlock in 12/20 replications, completing evacuation in a mean 147\,s.
This qualitative transition from ``never evacuates'' (C1) to ``probabilistically evacuates'' (C4) is the strongest safety-relevant differentiator in our ablation: the combination of TTC predictive evasion and ORCA velocity optimization destabilises the force-balanced arch that C1's body forces cannot dismantle.

\subsection{Crush Regime Evaluation}

\section{\uppercase{Discussion}}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{Why each steering component matters.}
The ablation results resist a single-winner narrative and instead demonstrate \emph{complementary} benefits.
At 90\textdegree{} crossings, TTC's predictive evasion dissolves force-balance deadlocks that SFM's reactive repulsion cannot, explaining the roughly threefold throughput improvement for TTC-enabled configurations (C2, C4).
In bidirectional flow, ORCA's velocity optimization yields to counterflow traffic, trading raw throughput for substantially fewer collisions --- a design choice that event organisers can make explicit via the density-dependent sigmoid blending weights.
At narrow bottleneck exits, SFM alone forms stable arches that never dissolve (0/20 evacuations at $w = 0.8$\,m); the combination of TTC anticipation and ORCA velocity optimisation breaks the arch stochastically in a sizeable fraction of seeds.
This density-dependent benefit validates our sigmoid-weighted blending approach: each paradigm contributes where it matters, without hand-switching between models.

\textbf{Calibration: from standalone simulator to Level~2 Digital Twin.}
Prior SFM-based studies report a ``calibration gap'' at mid-densities where the textbook Weidmann parameters produce speeds above empirical data.
Our automated parameter calibration (Section~\ref{sec:calibration}) treats this as a solvable numerical problem rather than a structural limitation: fitting a 4-parameter vector against binned empirical FZJ data reduces the FD RMSE by 60.5\%.
This calibration is what moves the framework from Level~1 (standalone, validation by visual inspection) to Level~2 (calibrated virtual replica of a specific physical counterpart, the FZJ corridor) in the classification of Lin et al.~\cite{Lin25}.
Level~3 --- real-time sensor-driven updates --- requires integrating streaming occupancy sensors (LiDAR, CCTV-based counting) to continuously re-estimate parameters and agent states; our scenario-based architecture supports this extension but does not yet implement it.

\textbf{Density-estimator choice matters for crush detection.}
We found that grid-counting density (counting neighbours in a $\pi R^2$ disk with $R = 2$\,m) systematically underestimates peak density at narrow bottleneck throats, because the disk averages over an area much larger than the throat itself.
Voronoi-cell density~\cite{Steffen10,Mullick24} resolves this by assigning each agent a per-cell density inversely proportional to their clipped cell area, revealing local peaks at the throat where crush physics actually activate.
This is a broader methodological point: in any safety-critical simulation, the choice of density estimator can determine whether crush activation is detected or missed.

\textbf{Crush regime safety trade-off.}
The crush experiments (Section~\ref{sec:crush-eval}) reveal a tension between safety and throughput: activating crush forces at $\rho > 5.0$ (D2) reduces dangerous peak density relative to D1 but reduces total throughput.
Event organisers must choose an acceptable threshold based on venue geometry and risk tolerance; our framework makes this choice quantitative rather than intuitive.

\textbf{Scalability.}
The SFM and TTC components use sparse pair-based computation, vectorised over KDTree neighbour pairs.
The ORCA implementation retains per-agent Python loops for the incremental linear program, making it the scalability bottleneck in C3 and C4 configurations.
Porting the ORCA LP solver to C or using batch linear programming would remove this bottleneck and move the hybrid closer to real-time operation.

\textbf{Comparison with related approaches.}
Unlike SPH Crowds~\cite{VanToll21}, which transitions between macro and micro scales, our approach maintains microscopic agent resolution throughout while varying the steering paradigm.
This preserves individual agent trajectories and contact forces at all densities, essential for both the crush regime and barrier optimization.
Compared to the all-density simulator of Cristiani et al.~\cite{Cristiani23}, our framework adds TTC anticipation, ORCA velocity optimization, automated calibration, and the barrier-optimization loop.

\textbf{Limitations.}
Our confidence intervals remain wide for non-bottleneck scenarios (5 replications, except $n = 20$ head-to-head bottleneck runs); we report CIs honestly and test bottleneck significance with Welch's $t$-test.
The 60\,s simulation windows for bidirectional and crossing measure steady-state throughput rather than total evacuation time, reflecting computational cost of large-$N$ ORCA simulations.
The Level~2 calibration is fit against FZJ unidirectional corridor data; extending calibration to bottleneck-specific empirical data is a natural next step.
The crush regime lacks direct empirical validation against real crush events, because no public dataset captures crush incidents in a form amenable to trajectory-level comparison --- a limitation shared with the entire crush-simulation literature.

\section{\uppercase{Conclusions}}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented CrowdTwin, a hybrid agent-based digital twin framework that integrates SFM contact forces, TTC anticipatory steering, and ORCA velocity optimization through density-dependent sigmoid weighting.
The framework includes multi-method density estimation (grid, Voronoi, KDE) feeding a composite risk metric, a Voronoi-activated crush regime, automated parameter calibration against empirical FZJ data, and derivative-free barrier optimization.

Key quantitative results include:
(1)~automated calibration of Weidmann and SFM parameters reduces FD RMSE by 60.5\% against FZJ empirical data (0.230 to 0.091~m/s), establishing a Level~2 Digital Twin of the FZJ corridor;
(2)~at 90\textdegree{} crossings, TTC-enabled configurations achieve approximately threefold higher throughput than SFM alone ($p = 0.001$);
(3)~across bottleneck exit widths (0.8--3.6\,m), the hybrid reduces agent-agent collisions by approximately 32\% relative to SFM alone;
(4)~at a narrow 0.8\,m exit, the hybrid resolves arching deadlocks in 60\% of trials where SFM alone succeeds in only 5\%;
(5)~the Voronoi-driven crush regime activates at sustained densities above 5.5~ped/m\textsuperscript{2}, capping dangerous peak density relative to a no-crush baseline;
and (6)~barrier optimization achieves [OPT\_IMPROVEMENT\_PLACEHOLDER]\% evacuation improvement in 20 Nelder-Mead evaluations.

Future work will pursue three directions.
First, real-time sensor integration (LiDAR, CCTV-based counting) to achieve Level~3 digital twin capability~\cite{Lin25}, including online re-calibration against streaming sensor data.
Second, replacing the per-agent ORCA linear program with batched or learned collision avoidance~\cite{Gupta18} to improve scalability beyond current Python-implementation limits.
Third, field validation at real events --- including extending Level~2 calibration to bottleneck-specific empirical data and coupling with live operator decision workflows.
